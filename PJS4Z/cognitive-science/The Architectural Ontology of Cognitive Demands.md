# The Architectural Ontology of Cognitive Demands: A Comprehensive Analysis of Frameworks and Task Taxonomies

The systematic categorization of mental processes, behavioral actions, and their operational requirements—broadly conceptualized as the architectural ontology of cognitive demands—represents a pivotal intersection of cognitive science, neuroscience, systems engineering, educational psychology, and informatics. Scientific ontologies provide the essential knowledge structures that define the specific entities within a given domain, delineate their properties, and establish the complex, agreed-upon relationships between them. In the behavioral and cognitive sciences, developing these structured frameworks is critical to moving beyond isolated experimental paradigms toward generalized, predictive models of human performance, learning, and systemic interaction.

To fully understand the architecture of task demands, researchers across multiple disciplines have historically developed highly specialized, sometimes competing, theoretical frameworks. These frameworks map how latent cognitive constructs—such as working memory, selective attention, and executive control—are recruited, utilized, and constrained by external task environments. The resulting ontologies range from purely computational models designed to mathematically simulate human decision-making, to ecological frameworks mapping the sociotechnical boundaries of physical work, to neuro-semantic knowledge graphs that bridge abstract psychological constructs with functional neuroanatomy. This comprehensive dossier examines the leading frameworks rooted in cognitive science, detailing their origins, the upstream theories that laid their groundwork, and the downstream disciplines that apply them to solve complex real-world problems.

## Computational and Information-Processing Architectures

The effort to represent human cognition mathematically and computationally laid the essential groundwork for modern artificial intelligence, cognitive ergonomics, and human-computer interaction (HCI). These frameworks attempt to replicate the exact procedural and declarative memory demands required for an operator to execute complex tasks, operating under the assumption that the mind functions analogously to an information-processing system.

### The Model Human Processor (MHP) and GOMS

The Model Human Processor (MHP) and its associated task analysis methodology, GOMS (Goals, Operators, Methods, and Selection rules), represent early, highly influential attempts to quantify the architectural ontology of cognitive tasks within interactive digital environments. These models conceptualize human architecture as a series of interconnected processors—specifically perceptual, cognitive, and motor processors—interacting with a multi-layered memory system that includes both short-term working memory and long-term recall.

The MHP and the GOMS framework were introduced by the salient academic figures Stuart K. Card, Thomas P. Moran, and Allen Newell in their seminal 1983 text, _The Psychology of Human-Computer Interaction_. The framework emerged from an ambitious attempt to synthesize the state of psychological understanding regarding immediate behavioral responses into a calculable, predictive model of human interaction.

The theoretical roots and upstream influences of the MHP and GOMS lie deeply embedded in early information-processing psychology and experimental chronometry. A fundamental upstream influence is Hick’s Law, formulated by William Edmund Hick in 1952, which mathematically describes the time it takes for a person to make a decision as a logarithmic function of the possible choices available. By integrating Hick's Law with other cognitive principles, the MHP sought to quantify the precise millisecond timing of internal operators, such as the time required to map a stimulus to a response. Furthermore, the conceptualization of memory within the MHP is a direct inheritance from the multi-store memory models prominent in the 1960s and 1970s.

Downstream, GOMS and the MHP have become universally foundational to the discipline of Human-Computer Interaction (HCI) and graphical user interface design. GOMS analyses provide quantitative performance estimates for simple reasoning and memory retrieval, allowing software designers to predict user behavior, efficiency, and learning curves before a system is physically built. In advanced contemporary applications, researchers utilize refined GOMS-style models to predict complex human errors in high-stakes environments. For instance, these models are used to predict "habit capture errors," which occur when the human cognitive architecture fails to allocate sufficient resources to retrieve task-relevant information from long-term memory. Lacking the unretrieved information, the operator's decision mechanisms act in accordance with implicit default assumptions, resulting in catastrophic errors when those relied-upon assumptions prove incorrect.

### ACT-R, SOAR, and the Standard Model of the Mind

While GOMS focuses primarily on routine human-computer interaction, broader cognitive architectures attempt to model the entirety of general human intelligence and complex problem-solving. ACT-R (Adaptive Control of Thought-Rational) and SOAR (State, Operator, and Result) are the premier cognitive architectures that define the structural demands of high-level cognition.

John R. Anderson developed ACT-R in the early 1990s as a hybrid cognitive architecture integrating both symbolic representation and sub-symbolic neural-like mechanisms. Concurrently, SOAR was developed by John Laird, Allen Newell, and Paul Rosenbloom, functioning as a comprehensive architecture for general intelligence. Together with SIGMA (developed by Rosenbloom, Demski, and Ustun in 2016), these architectures form what Laird, Lebiere, and Rosenbloom formalized in 2017 as the "Standard Model of the Mind," an effort to unify cognitive architectures in a manner akin to the Standard Model in physics.

The architectural ontology of ACT-R and SOAR is deeply rooted in the General Problem Solver (GPS), an upstream computational model developed by Allen Newell and Herbert A. Simon in 1959. Furthermore, Herbert Simon’s foundational concept of "bounded rationality" (1955)—the notion that human cognition operates under strict computational limits, time constraints, and incomplete knowledge due to evolutionary heritage—serves as the primary theoretical paradigm dictating how these models manage task demands.

These architectures are heavily utilized in downstream applications spanning artificial intelligence, cognitive ergonomics, and complex task simulation. For example, ACT-R has been successfully utilized to model aviation crew performance, systematically simulating the distinct, overlapping, and highly demanding duties of a Pilot Flying (PF) and a Pilot Not Flying (PNF) under varying task loads and unexpected air traffic control interruptions. In the realm of artificial intelligence, SOAR and ACT-R are increasingly integrated with neuro-symbolic algorithms and large language models (such as OlaGPT) to enhance generalized planning abilities. By grounding AI in these cognitive architectures, researchers allow deep learning models to inherit human-like systematic generalization and bounded reasoning capabilities, overcoming the limitations of purely statistical, GPU-accelerated computing systems.

## Ecological and Sociotechnical Systems Frameworks

While computational architectures model the internal mechanisms and limitations of cognition in a vacuum, ecological frameworks model how cognition behaves when structurally coupled with complex, dynamic, and unpredictable environments. These frameworks argue that the ontology of a cognitive demand cannot be separated from the sociotechnical constraints of the physical workspace in which the task is performed.

### Cognitive Work Analysis (CWA) and the SRK Framework

Cognitive Work Analysis (CWA) and the accompanying Skill-Rule-Knowledge (SRK) framework represent a paradigm shift in human factors engineering, moving the analytical focus away from mapping internal mental states toward mapping the constraints of the work domain that ultimately shape cognitive behavior.

CWA and the SRK framework were spearheaded by the salient academic figure Jens Rasmussen in the 1980s and further formalized into a comprehensive engineering methodology by Kim J. Vicente in 1999. Rasmussen's SRK taxonomy provides a robust ontology by categorizing cognitive control and task demands across three distinct levels of abstraction :

|**Control Level**|**Behavioral Characteristics**|**Interface Requirement**|**Domain Application Example**|
|---|---|---|---|
|**Skill-Based Behavior**|Smooth, highly integrated, and automated sensory-motor performance executed without conscious control.|Temporal and spatial continuous signals that support unbroken perception-action loops.|Manually adjusting a continuous flow valve to maintain pressure.|
|**Rule-Based Behavior**|Goal-oriented performance driven by stored rules or procedures, triggered by familiar environmental signs.|Clear "signs" that map directly to established action rules and procedural manuals.|Responding to a standard warning light by initiating a shutdown sequence.|
|**Knowledge-Based Behavior**|Analytical problem-solving in unfamiliar, unpredicted situations, requiring the conscious formulation of new plans.|Complex "symbols" that accurately represent the internal functional topology and physics of the system.|Diagnosing an unpredicted multi-point reactor failure requiring novel interventions.|

The ontology of CWA heavily borrows from the upstream sociotechnical systems tradition and Soft Systems Methodology (SSM), assuming a constructivist perspective where individuals construct understanding through environmental interaction. Crucially, it integrates principles from Cybernetics and Egon Brunswik’s Ecological Psychology (the Lens Model). Rasmussen’s safety and migration model conceptually roots the framework, arguing that complex systems naturally drift toward the boundaries of acceptable performance under the constant pressure of cost-effectiveness and high workload. Because system designers cannot foresee all local contingencies, workers inherently modify procedures. CWA maps these boundaries, emphasizing the need for operators to develop "coping skills" at the edge of system safety. Additional upstream and parallel influences include Resilience Engineering (Woods and Hollnagel), the Functional Resonance Analysis Method (FRAM), and Systems-Theoretic Accident Model and Processes (STAMP) developed by Nancy Leveson.

CWA serves as the methodological backbone for downstream disciplines such as Ecological Interface Design (EID). EID is heavily utilized in safety-critical, high-stakes environments where unanticipated events demand high-level knowledge-based reasoning. Downstream applications include the design of human-machine interfaces for nuclear power plant control rooms, where CWA is used to create "responsibility maps" and "chained decision ladders" for Senior Nuclear Operators (SNO). It is also applied in military communications platforms, neonatal intensive care medicine, and railway network management. In these domains, the Abstraction Hierarchy (AH) aspect of Work Domain Analysis is used to align sensory cues with specific cognitive demands, ensuring that operators are not forced to engage in high-demand, error-prone knowledge-based reasoning when simpler rule-based processing would suffice.

## Cognitive Capacity, Workload, and Resource Allocation

Understanding the architectural ontology of tasks requires quantifying the absolute limitations of the cognitive system. Frameworks centered on cognitive workload explore how attention, working memory, and perceptual resources are allocated, depleted, and structurally isolated during complex task performance.

### Multiple Resource Theory (MRT)

The Multiple Resource Theory posits that human attention and cognitive processing are not fueled by a single, monolithic pool of mental energy, but rather by distinct, semi-independent resource pools. When concurrent tasks compete for the exact same specific resource, performance degrades and interference occurs; when tasks utilize separate resources, they can be performed simultaneously with minimal decrement.

Christopher D. Wickens is the primary academic figure associated with Multiple Resource Theory, synthesizing the framework over decades of research starting in the late 1970s and culminating in the highly influential 4-Dimensional Multiple Resource Model introduced in 2002 and refined in 2008.

The groundwork for MRT was laid by earlier upstream theories of attention and cognitive effort. Wickens specifically sought to refine Daniel Kahneman's single-resource capacity model, which struggled to explain why certain dual-task combinations were easier than others. Pivotal upstream experimental work by David Navon, Daniel Gopher, and Neville Moray in the late 1970s demonstrated that dual-task interference varied predictably based on explicit instructions, payoff schedules, and structural overlap, suggesting that humans possessed the architectural capacity to modulate the supply of distinct, separate resources.

Wickens' 4-Dimensional model evaluates task overlap across four distinct architectural dimensions to predict interference. Dual-task interference increases proportionally with the degree to which two tasks share these dimensions:

|**Resource Dimension**|**Dichotomy / Categories**|**Impact on Cognitive Demand**|
|---|---|---|
|**Processing Stages**|Perceptual/Cognitive vs. Response|Tasks requiring heavy perception/working memory (e.g., reading an instrument) will interfere minimally with tasks requiring pure motor response (e.g., steering).|
|**Sensory Modalities**|Auditory vs. Visual|Cross-modal time-sharing (listening to a voice while looking at a screen) is vastly superior to intra-modal time-sharing (looking at two different screens simultaneously).|
|**Representational Codes**|Spatial vs. Verbal|Spatial tasks (e.g., tracking a moving target) interfere heavily with other spatial tasks, but less so with verbal tasks (e.g., speaking or reading text).|
|**Visual Channels**|Focal vs. Ambient|Focal vision (used for detailed object recognition and reading) draws from a different resource pool than ambient vision (used for peripheral orientation and movement detection).|

The computational rendering of this multi-dimensional model has profound downstream applications in human factors engineering, specifically in predicting multitask workload overload. It is heavily utilized in aviation display design, driving simulator research, and the design of complex operator workstations. By mapping tasks onto these four dimensions, engineers can construct "conflict matrices" to identify precisely where simultaneous tasks will cause performance breakdowns due to structural resource competition, allowing for the proactive redesign of operational workflows and physical interfaces.

### Cognitive Load Theory (CLT)

While MRT focuses on task interference during concurrent operational execution, Cognitive Load Theory analyzes how the architecture of working memory inherently limits the acquisition, synthesis, and retention of complex, novel information during learning.

John Sweller formulated Cognitive Load Theory in 1988 after observing that conventional problem-solving methods often overwhelm a learner's short-term memory capacity, paradoxically hindering the acquisition of domain-specific knowledge. The framework divides cognitive demands into three distinct types: intrinsic load (the inherent complexity and interactivity of the material being learned), extraneous load (unnecessary cognitive burden imposed by poorly designed instructional formats), and germane load (the productive cognitive effort applied to creating and automating permanent schemas in long-term memory).

CLT is uniquely grounded in upstream evolutionary educational psychology, drawing heavily from the categorization of knowledge established by David Geary. Geary distinguished between _biologically primary knowledge_ and _biologically secondary knowledge_. Biologically primary knowledge consists of skills evolved over thousands of generations that are acquired effortlessly and unconsciously, such as learning to speak a native language, generalizing, or recognizing human faces. Because humans possess an evolved aptitude for these tasks, they impose virtually no cognitive load. Conversely, biologically secondary knowledge encompasses culturally important skills requiring conscious, intense effort, such as mathematics, reading, or complex software operation. CLT primarily addresses the architectural demands of secondary knowledge. Sweller equated human cognitive architecture to a "natural information processing system" operating under the exact same principles as biological evolution by natural selection. This involves the "information store principle" (analogous to long-term memory), the "borrowing and reorganizing principle," and "randomness as genesis" (processing novel information in a strictly limited working memory).

CLT is the predominant theoretical driver in downstream instructional design and educational technology. Downstream frameworks rely heavily on its principles to structure modern curricula. Notably, it underlies the Four Component Instructional Design (4C/ID) model developed by Van Merriënboer (1997), as well as the multimedia learning principles of Mayer and Moreno. These downstream applications dictate how educators and interface designers can minimize extraneous cognitive load through specific, empirically validated techniques, such as the split-attention effect, the redundancy effect, and the use of process worksheets and worked examples.

## Structural Ontologies of Executive Control

As functional neuroimaging and advanced psychometrics matured, researchers sought to anchor abstract behavioral taxonomies to precise, quantifiable physiological and statistical substrates. These frameworks attempt to map the architectural ontology of cognition onto specific brain networks and latent statistical variables, addressing the longstanding tension between unified general intelligence and diverse, highly specialized mental modules.

### The Unity/Diversity Framework of Executive Functions

Executive functions (EFs) are the top-down cognitive processes required for goal-directed behavior, planning, and self-regulation. Understanding whether these functions operate as a single unified mechanism or as distinct, independent cognitive modules has been a central challenge in mapping the architecture of the mind.

Akira Miyake and Naomi Friedman authored a landmark 2000 paper that utilized confirmatory factor analysis (CFA) to definitively map the structural relations of executive functions. Through rigorous statistical modeling, they identified three foundational, separable components of EF: mental set shifting (switching flexibly between tasks or mental states), information updating and monitoring (constantly revising the contents of working memory), and the inhibition of prepotent responses (deliberately suppressing automatic or dominant behaviors). In 2012, they expanded this initial finding into the definitive "Unity/Diversity Framework," demonstrating through structural equation modeling that a "Common EF" latent variable exists (representing unity, or the shared variance among all tasks), alongside nested, statistically separable latent variables specific to updating and shifting (representing diversity).

Miyake and Friedman's work originated as a direct methodological solution to the upstream "task impurity problem" inherent in clinical neuropsychology. Historically, researchers relied on classic, highly complex "frontal lobe" tests—such as the Wisconsin Card Sorting Test (WCST), the Tower of Hanoi (TOH), and various operation span tasks—to measure executive function. However, performance on these tasks relies on multiple underlying cognitive processes simultaneously, confounding the precise measurement of any single cognitive demand. By applying latent variable analysis to extract the shared variance across multiple, procedurally diverse tasks, the framework successfully isolated the core architectural components of EF from the noise of the specific testing apparatus. More recent upstream critiques, such as those by Sambol et al. (2023), have engaged with this framework, cautioning that overly similar procedural tasks can artificially inflate structural correlations, further refining the ontology of EF testing.

The Unity/Diversity model is a foundational cornerstone in downstream disciplines such as individual differences research, developmental psychology, and behavioral genetics. It has profoundly influenced how researchers evaluate cognitive deficits, proving that while general executive dysfunction impacts overall task performance, specific structural impairments correlate differentially with clinical outcomes. For example, the framework has been utilized to map developmental trajectories in preadolescent children versus older adults, and it is heavily cited in studies exploring the etiology of ADHD, predicting variations in IQ, and establishing the neural correlates of cognitive aging.

## Neural Network Ontologies of Cognitive Control

Paralleling the psychometric mapping of executive functions, cognitive neuroscience has established anatomical ontologies for top-down task control, locating the precise neurological networks responsible for managing cognitive demands.

### The Multiple-Demand (MD) Network

The Multiple-Demand (MD) Network provides a robust anatomical ontology for general cognitive effort, identifying the specific brain structures recruited when tasks become difficult or novel.

John Duncan is the primary academic figure who synthesized the concept of the Multiple-Demand system, notably in a highly influential 2010 publication. The framework identifies a specific, widespread fronto-parietal network—including the lateral prefrontal cortex, the intraparietal sulcus, the anterior cingulate cortex, and the anterior insula—that universally activates in response to task difficulty and complex cognitive demands. Remarkably, the MD network activates regardless of the specific sensory modality (e.g., visual versus auditory) or the specific behavioral output required by the task.

The MD framework is heavily influenced by earlier upstream models of visual attention and historical constructs of general intelligence. Duncan’s earlier work with Robert Desimone in 1995 on the "biased competition" model of attention laid the critical groundwork. This model posited that top-down signals originating from the prefrontal cortex bias the competition for representation in upstream sensory cortices, effectively dictating which stimuli gain cognitive focus.Furthermore, the network is frequently conceptualized as the physical neural instantiation of Charles Spearman's _g_(general intelligence), acting as a superordinate cognitive control network that constructs mental programs for novel, intelligent behavior. Related upstream work on the "Cognitive Control Network" (CCN) by researchers like Michael Cole suggests that these prefrontal regions act as "flexible hubs," rapidly updating their global functional connectivity patterns to adapt to shifting task demands.

The MD framework is ubiquitous in downstream functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) research, offering a vital baseline for evaluating how task demands scale neural activation. Recent downstream applications have bridged cognitive neuroscience with artificial intelligence and linguistics. For instance, researchers utilize the MD network to interpret the processing mechanics of natural language processing models. Studies mapping BERT structural probing representations against human fMRI data have revealed that the left prefrontal regions of the MD network activate heavily when human listeners resolve complex syntactic ambiguities (e.g., differentiating passive versus active verbs in real-time). This mirrors the immense working memory demands required to hold open various structural nodes while building the architecture of an unfolding sentence.

In clinical neurology, downstream researchers utilize "activity flow modeling" over the MD and intrinsic resting-state networks to predict aberrant task activations in early-stage Alzheimer's disease. By mapping how healthy task activations flow over altered functional connections, researchers can predict cognitive dysfunction in at-risk individuals without requiring them to perform demanding in-scanner cognitive tasks. Furthermore, advanced 7-Tesla fMRI studies utilize the MD network to pinpoint the exact neural substrates of the "central bottleneck" of multitasking, providing direct neural evidence that the fronto-parietal network strictly enforces serial queuing of information processing when humans attempt concurrent, highly demanding tasks.

## Neuroinformatics and Semantic Web Ontologies

A fundamental, pervasive problem in mapping task ontologies across disciplines is semantic ambiguity. Different research traditions frequently use identical terms to describe distinct cognitive processes, or conversely, use radically different terms to describe the exact same neurological phenomenon. To create a rigorous, machine-readable architecture for the future of cognitive science, the field has turned to formal bioinformatics.

### The Cognitive Atlas and Cognitive Ontologies

The exponential volume of neuroimaging data generated in the 21st century necessitated a structured, formalized framework to reliably connect empirical brain observations to theoretical psychological constructs.

Russell A. Poldrack established the Cognitive Atlas project, an open, collaborative knowledge base aiming to develop a strict formal ontology for cognitive neuroscience. Poldrack identified that cumulative progress in the discipline was severely bottlenecked by a lack of "semantic infrastructure". Historically, researchers were frequently confounding abstract psychological constructs (e.g., the concept of "working memory") with the specific operational tasks used to measure them (e.g., the "Sternberg task"). This produced vast confusion in the literature, as a single task invariably involves multiple constructs, and terms like "active maintenance" and "short-term memory" were used interchangeably across different laboratories.

The Cognitive Atlas is fundamentally inspired by the massive success of large-scale biomedical ontologies, explicitly the Gene Ontology, which standardized the representation of gene functions. Furthermore, it relies heavily on the upstream architecture of the Semantic Web and the OBO (Open Biomedical Ontologies) Relational Ontology. By leveraging standardized semantic web technologies like RDF (Resource Description Framework) and OWL (Web Ontology Language), the project ensures that the mapping of cognition is inherently machine-processable and seamlessly queryable via SPARQL endpoints.

The structural ontology of the Cognitive Atlas rigidly disambiguates cognitive science by separating latent Mental Concepts from observable Mental Tasks, bridging them with formalized, machine-readable relationships. The ontology utilizes both standard relational parameters and novel, domain-specific linkages:

|**Ontological Relation**|**Type of Semantic Link**|**Example within Cognitive Architecture**|
|---|---|---|
|**is-a**|Standard hierarchical classification.|"Declarative memory _is-a_ kind of memory."|
|**part-of**|Compositional or structural inclusion.|"Memory retrieval is a _part-of_ declarative memory."|
|**preceded-by**|Temporal or sequential dependency.|"Memory consolidation is _preceded-by_ memory encoding."|
|**measured-by**|Domain-specific empirical bridge.|"Conflict processing is _measured-by_ the incongruent–congruent contrast in a Stroop task."|
|**descended-from**|Task phylogeny and historical evolution.|Tracks the evolution of various iterations of the Color-Word Stroop task over decades.|

This formalized framework is highly salient in downstream disciplines such as large-scale neuroimaging meta-analysis, connectomics, and psychiatric phenomics. By formalizing the ontology, downstream researchers can utilize Encoding Models (EMs) to accurately predict brain-wide neocortical activation patterns for arbitrary, unmapped psychological tasks simply by referencing the established ontological functions known to be engaged by the new task, outperforming traditional permutation-based null models. Furthermore, it allows researchers in Genome-Wide Association Studies (GWAS) to systematically link specific cognitive indicators to vast phenotypic expressions, bypassing the labile and inconsistent behavioral labeling historically used in cognitive psychology to find the true genetic signals underlying neuropsychiatric syndromes.

## Applied Taxonomies of Human Abilities and Learning

At the furthest downstream applied level, the architectural ontology of cognitive demands is translated into operational, heuristic taxonomies utilized in education, personnel selection, and industrial-organizational psychology. These frameworks abandon complex neuro-mechanistic modeling in favor of highly pragmatic classification systems designed to predict macro-level behavioral output and learning success.

### Bloom’s Taxonomy of Educational Objectives

Bloom's Taxonomy is universally recognized as the preeminent heuristic framework for defining the architectural cognitive demands required for deep knowledge acquisition, mastery, and assessment.

Created by Benjamin Bloom and a committee of psychological collaborators in 1956, the original taxonomy classified cognitive demands into a rigid pyramid of nouns: Knowledge, Comprehension, Application, Analysis, Synthesis, and Evaluation. However, in 2001, David Krathwohl (one of Bloom's original collaborators) and educational researcher Lorin Anderson spearheaded a massive structural revision. This revision fundamentally altered the architectural ontology of the framework by shifting the static nouns to active verbs (Remembering, Understanding, Applying, Analyzing, Evaluating, Creating), placing "Creating" at the apex of cognitive complexity. Crucially, the 2001 revision separated the cognitive process from the knowledge dimension, specifying that a learner can apply different cognitive processes to four distinct architectural types of knowledge: factual, conceptual, procedural, and metacognitive.

The 1956 iteration was deeply embedded in the upstream behavioral psychology paradigms of the mid-20th century, which viewed learning as passive acquisition. The 2001 revision integrated decades of upstream advancements in cognitive psychology and curriculum theory, shifting the paradigm toward the active, constructivist performance of complex learning tasks.

Bloom’s Taxonomy dominates downstream curriculum design, psychometrics, and the learning sciences. It acts as the foundational lexicon of actionable verbs for mapping course objectives directly to assessment mechanisms. Modern downstream instructional design paradigms, such as Backward Design (popularized by Wiggins and McTighe) and Universal Design for Learning (UDL), rely explicitly on the taxonomy to ensure that selected learning activities align with valid, scientifically rigorous educational purposes rather than relying on traditionally unstructured, intuition-based design approaches.

### Fleishman’s Taxonomy of Human Abilities

Where Bloom focuses on academic and conceptual learning, other taxonomies focus on identifying the absolute capacity limits required for physical and cognitive labor across industrial environments.

Edwin A. Fleishman developed the rigorous Taxonomy of Human Abilities, culminating in his comprehensive 1984 publication _Taxonomies of Human Performance_ (co-authored with Quaintance and Broedling). Based on decades of extensive empirical research and large-scale observation, Fleishman identified exactly 52 distinct human abilities spanning cognitive, psychomotor, and physical domains that act as the foundational requirements to perform a broad spectrum of human tasks.

The methodology behind Fleishman’s taxonomy relies heavily on the psychometric traditions established upstream by Alfred Binet, Theodore Simon, and Charles Spearman in the early 20th century. By utilizing large-scale factor analysis—the exact same mathematical techniques used by Spearman to derive general intelligence (_g_)—Fleishman sought to isolate the precise, unvarying physiological and cognitive factors that act as the structural, biological limits of human performance.

Fleishman’s taxonomy is deeply integrated into downstream Industrial-Organizational (I-O) psychology and physical ergonomics. It is universally used in task and job analysis, directly informing widespread assessment tools like the Position Analysis Questionnaire (PAQ). By calibrating the precise numerical amount of an ability required for a task—for example, empirically identifying that "oral expression" is highly salient for corporate negotiation, or that a specific threshold of "selective attention" is mandatory for radar monitoring—systems engineers and human resource professionals can accurately predict individual differences in task performance. This allows for the design of highly targeted personnel selection rubrics, safety interventions, and specialized military training regimens.

### Cognitive Task Analysis (CTA)

Cognitive Task Analysis bridges the gap between the rigid categorization of psychometric taxonomies (like Fleishman's) and the fluid, dynamic, and often opaque nature of real-world operational expertise.

Spearheaded by researchers such as Robert Hoffman and Laura Militello, CTA emerged as a diverse family of psychological research methods designed specifically to unpack the internal, unobservable thought processes of domain experts. CTA extends the architectural ontology of traditional behavioral task analysis. While older upstream methods focused strictly on observable actions, time-and-motion studies, and physical interface interactions, CTA evolved to tap into the unobservable mental mechanics underlying high-level behavior—specifically how experts frame ambiguous problems, interpret subtle environmental cues, and evaluate competing goals under extreme time pressure.

CTA is heavily utilized in downstream human factors engineering, instructional design, and the architecture of autonomous systems. Methodological enhancements like Cognitive Task Analysis and Workload Classification (CTAWC) provide standardized frameworks to decompose cognitive tasks, utilizing existing taxonomies to precisely identify sources of cognitive workload. As modern automation increasingly relieves humans of repetitive physical tasks, human operators are forced into novel supervisory roles requiring high-level, continuous cognitive monitoring of autonomous agents. CTA is utilized to map the invisible cognitive demands of these new supervisory roles, directly informing the development of safer human-machine interfaces, advanced pupillometry assessments, and robust cultural competence models used in international relations and human resources.

## The Architectural Ontology of the Built Environment

While the aforementioned frameworks apply the ontology of cognitive demands to digital interfaces, educational curricula, and industrial operations, an emerging discipline applies these exact principles to physical space: the architectural ontology of the built environment.

Architectural theorists such as Patrik Schumacher, alongside researchers like A. Aksamija and F. Grobler, have advanced the concept that physical architecture must be understood not just as a structural or aesthetic exercise, but as a system that imposes and resolves specific cognitive and perceptual demands. Schumacher theorizes architectural ontology through multiple dimensions, including the "task dimension," the "phenomenological project," and the "semiological project". In this framework, a building acts as a physical, perceptual, and communicative frame that explicitly engineers the engagement of users as cognitive beings and socialized actors, soliciting specific active behaviors and passive movements through spatial design.

This approach draws upon upstream insights from environmental psychology, which identifies how spatial factors—such as light, color, and circulatory patterns—shape visual reactions and dictate behavioral patterns within the built environment. Furthermore, it relies on mathematical frameworks like "shape grammars" (pioneered by George Stiny in 1980), which capture the spatial and topological aspects of building styles to make the cognitive task of generative building design significantly more tractable.

Downstream, this cognitive-architectural ontology is applied to advanced spatial planning and facilities management. For instance, researchers utilize architectural ontology concepts combined with multidimensional log data from Hospital Information Systems (HIS) to predict complex spatial demands in healthcare buildings. By mapping the cognitive and physical workflow of medical personnel against the ontological constraints of the hospital's architecture, designers can achieve highly efficient, lightweight spatial demand prediction, adapting complex research scenarios into functional, optimized real-world environments.

## Conclusion

The architectural ontology of cognitive demands is not unified under a single, monolithic theory. Rather, it represents a highly stratified collection of frameworks, each tailored to the specific epistemic priorities and operational scales of differing scientific disciplines.

At the microscopic and computational level, architectures like ACT-R and SOAR mathematically simulate the bounded constraints of human memory and processing to inform the next generation of neuro-symbolic artificial intelligence. At the physiological level, models like the Multiple-Demand Network and the Unity/Diversity Framework map abstract psychometric concepts—like executive control and general intelligence—onto specific structural topologies in the prefrontal cortex, aiding in advanced neuro-clinical diagnostics and Alzheimer's prediction. Meanwhile, sociotechnical paradigms like Cognitive Work Analysis reject the isolation of the mind entirely, insisting that task demands are ecologically negotiated between the worker and the physical constraints of complex systems.

Ultimately, the future of this domain lies in the successful interoperability of these frameworks. Semantic technologies like the Cognitive Atlas are currently undertaking the massive effort of standardizing the nomenclature across these diverse fields. By establishing machine-readable ontologies that seamlessly connect physiological brain data, computational simulation mechanisms, and observable applied task taxonomies, researchers can finally integrate localized, domain-specific findings into a cohesive, universally predictive science of human cognition.
