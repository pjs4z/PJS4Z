# 300 Frame Semantics Meta-Model of Cognition

Traditional Aristotelian taxonomies (e.g., "Working Memory *is a* type of Memory") fail to capture the theory-laden and relational complexity of cognitive science. A "representation" in cognitivism is fundamentally incompatible with a "representation" in connectionism.

To properly map canonical ontology concepts, we utilize a hierarchical meta-model rooted in **Frame Semantics** (developed by linguist Charles Fillmore). In this model, concepts (**Lexical Units**) are understood entirely by the specific functional roles (**Frame Elements**) they play within structured conceptual situations (**Frames**).

## The Root Frame: `COGNITIVE_EVENT`
Every concept in cognitive science ultimately inherits from this abstract root event.
- **Core Frame Elements (FEs):**
  - `[COGNIZER]`: The biological, artificial, or extended agent.
  - `[ENVIRONMENT]`: The contextual domain of interaction or problem space.
  - `[STATE]`: The internal condition, dynamics, or representational structure of the Cognizer.
  - `[PROCESS]`: The interaction, computation, or coupling occurring over time.

## Hierarchical Sub-Frames (The Taxonomy)
Because the [[200 Theoretical Frameworks of Cognitive Science]] view the `COGNITIVE_EVENT` in fundamentally incompatible ways, our ontology bifurcates into four primary operational sub-frames. We map canonical concepts into these frames based on the roles they fulfill:

1. **[[301 Frame - Information Processing]]** (Structures the Cognitivist ontology)
2. **[[302 Frame - Network Dynamics]]** (Structures the Connectionist ontology)
3. **[[303 Frame - Embodied Action]]** (Structures the 4E ontology)
4. **[[304 Frame - Predictive Inference]]** (Structures the Predictive Processing ontology)
