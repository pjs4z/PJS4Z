I. STATE OF THE FRONTIER

Amphetamine and methamphetamine use disorders occupy a uniquely challenging position in addiction science. Unlike opioid use disorder, for which multiple FDA-approved pharmacotherapies exist, stimulant use disorder has no approved medication as of early 2026. The most promising pharmacological result to date—a combination of injectable naltrexone and oral bupropion—achieved a response rate of only 16.5% in a Phase III trial (Trivedi et al., 2021, NEJM). This therapeutic vacuum has forced the field to draw on a remarkably broad coalition of research traditions, from molecular neuropharmacology to contemplative neuroscience, from computational reinforcement learning to community-based recovery capital models.

The frontier is defined by several convergent insights. First, amphetamines produce profound and persistent neuroadaptations in the dopaminergic system—particularly dopamine transporter downregulation in the striatum—that show partial but meaningful recovery with protracted abstinence (Volkow et al., 2001). Second, the subjective experience of addiction is increasingly understood not as a monolithic "hijacked brain" but as a dynamic interplay between sensitized incentive motivation ("wanting" without proportionate "liking"), allostatic dysregulation of hedonic set points, the progressive consolidation of drug-seeking habits, and impaired prefrontal executive control. Third, computational psychiatry is beginning to formalize these processes as aberrant reinforcement learning algorithms, opening the door to precision diagnostics and individually-tailored interventions. Fourth, the most effective behavioral intervention for stimulant addiction—contingency management—works precisely by restoring the reward signal for non-drug reinforcers, yet remains drastically underdeployed due to policy and economic barriers.

The landscape is further enriched by emerging paradigms: mindfulness-based relapse prevention, which targets the craving-behavior link at its operant root; trauma-informed models linking adverse childhood experiences to dopaminergic vulnerability; recovery capital and identity-transformation frameworks from sociology; neuromodulation approaches including transcranial magnetic stimulation and deep brain stimulation; and the nascent exploration of psychedelic-assisted therapy for stimulant disorders. Each of these represents a distinct semantic frame—a way of conceptualizing what addiction is, what recovery means, and where intervention should target. The topology of these frames is the subject of this report.

II. TOPOLOGY OF SEMANTIC FRAMES

The 22 figures profiled in this report can be organized into four major clusters, each containing multiple complementary but distinct semantic frames. These clusters are not hermetically sealed—many researchers operate at their intersections—but they represent meaningfully different ontological commitments about the nature of addiction and the locus of recovery.

Cluster A: Neurobiological Mechanism Frames

These frames locate addiction’s essence in specific neural circuit dysfunctions. The Incentive Sensitization frame (Berridge & Robinson) holds that repeated amphetamine exposure produces lasting hypersensitivity in mesolimbic dopamine systems, amplifying "wanting" for drugs without increasing "liking." The Allostatic/Opponent-Process frame (Koob & Le Moal) emphasizes the progressive downregulation of reward circuits and recruitment of brain stress systems (CRF, dynorphin), creating a negative emotional state that drives compulsive use through negative reinforcement. The Dopaminergic Neuroimaging frame (Volkow) integrates PET evidence showing how stimulant-induced dopamine system changes impair frontal cortical circuits for self-control. The Synaptic Plasticity frame (Bhatt, Bhatt, Bhatt) examines the glutamatergic and dopaminergic molecular mechanisms through which amphetamines rewrite synaptic architecture. The Impaired Response Inhibition and Salience Attribution (iRISA) frame (Goldstein & Volkow) models addiction as the conjunction of hyperactive salience circuits and hypoactive inhibitory control.

Cluster B: Learning, Habit & Computational Frames

These frames conceptualize addiction as a disorder of learning systems. The Habit/Compulsion Transition frame (Everitt & Robbins) models addiction as a shift from goal-directed to habitual drug seeking, underpinned by a ventral-to-dorsal striatal progression. The Computational Reinforcement Learning frame (Redish; Daw; Huys) formalizes addiction as aberrant prediction error signaling and a pathological shift from model-based to model-free behavioral control. The Temporal Discounting/Behavioral Economics frame (Bickel; MacKillop) treats addiction as an extreme form of impulsive choice, where the steep temporal discounting of future rewards makes immediate drug reinforcement dominate decision-making. The Ersche Endophenotype frame identifies pre-existing neurocognitive vulnerabilities—impulsivity, compulsivity, executive dysfunction—as endophenotypes that precede and predict stimulant addiction.

Cluster C: Clinical Intervention & Recovery Frames

These frames are organized around specific treatment modalities. The Contingency Management frame (Higgins; Petry) applies operant conditioning principles to restore competing non-drug reinforcement, representing the strongest evidence base for stimulant addiction treatment. The Mindfulness-Based Relapse Prevention frame (Brewer; Bowen) targets the craving-behavior link by training meta-cognitive awareness of affective states, decoupling urge from action. The Cognitive Behavioral/Relapse Prevention frame (Carroll; Marlatt) emphasizes coping skill acquisition, cognitive restructuring, and functional analysis of use episodes. The Motivational Interviewing/Stages of Change frame (Miller & Rollnick; Prochaska & DiClemente) treats motivation as the central therapeutic variable, working with ambivalence rather than

against it. The Pharmacological Rescue frame (Trivedi; Ling) pursues pharmacological interventions to normalize dopaminergic dysfunction during recovery. The Neuromodulation frame (Hanlon; Ekhtiari) uses TMS and other brain stimulation technologies to directly modulate craving and executive control circuits.

Cluster D: Psychosocial, Ecological & Identity Frames

These frames locate recovery in social context and self-concept. The Recovery Capital frame (White; Cloud & Granfield) defines recovery as the accumulation of internal and external resources—social, physical, human, and cultural capital—rather than merely the cessation of use. The Self-Determination Theory frame (Ryan & Deci; applied by Zuroff and others) holds that sustainable recovery requires satisfaction of the basic psychological needs for autonomy, competence, and relatedness. The Trauma-Informed/ACE frame (Felitti; Maté) reframes addiction as a response to developmental trauma, requiring healing of the wound rather than management of the symptom. The Social Identity Model of Recovery (SIMOR) frame (Best; Beckwith) treats recovery as a process of identity transition, where the individual’s shift from an "addict" to a "person in recovery" identity is mediated by social group membership.

Key Tensions: The most productive tensions in the field run between Cluster A (mechanism) and Cluster D (context)—is addiction fundamentally a brain state or a social condition? Between Cluster B (learning/habit) and the Incentive Sensitization frame—is compulsive use habitual or motivationally driven? And between the Contingency Management frame (external reinforcement) and the Self-Determination frame (intrinsic motivation)—does extrinsic reward scaffolding undermine or enable autonomous recovery?

III. EXPERT PROFILES

CLUSTER A: NEUROBIOLOGICAL MECHANISM FRAMES

1. Kent C. Berridge

University of Michigan, Department of Psychology | Behavioral Neuroscience / Affective Neuroscience

Semantic Frame: Incentive Sensitization — 'Wanting' vs. 'Liking'

Seminal Work: Robinson, T.E. & Berridge, K.C. (1993). 'The neural basis of drug craving: An incentive-sensitization theory of addiction.' Brain Research Reviews, 18(3), 247–291. Updated in Annual Review of Psychology (2025).

Synthesis: Berridge and Robinson proposed that addictive drugs produce lasting sensitization of mesolimbic dopamine systems that mediate incentive salience ('wanting'), a motivational process dissociable from hedonic impact ('liking'). Repeated amphetamine exposure hyper-sensitizes these circuits, such that drug cues trigger compulsive 'wanting' even as the subjective pleasure of the drug diminishes. The theory explains the paradox at the heart of stimulant addiction: people desperately pursue a substance that no longer delivers proportionate pleasure. Crucially, sensitization persists for months or years after drug cessation, mediated by structural changes in nucleus accumbens and prefrontal cortical neurons.

Q1: What is the fundamental computational function of dopamine in the reward circuit, and how does this differ from the folk-psychological assumption?

Dopamine's primary role is not to generate pleasure but to attribute incentive salience—to transform a neutral perceptual representation of a stimulus into something motivationally 'wanted.' This 'wanting' is a specific psychological process that can be measured independently from hedonic 'liking.' The folk equation of dopamine with pleasure is empirically wrong: dopamine-depleted animals still show normal hedonic reactions to sweet tastes, even though they lose all motivation to pursue rewards.

Q2: How does the sensitization mechanism explain the chronicity and relapse vulnerability of amphetamine addiction specifically?

Amphetamines are among the most potent sensitizers of mesolimbic systems. Repeated intermittent exposure produces neural changes—including increased dopamine release, dendritic branching in accumbens neurons, and altered gene expression—that are remarkably persistent, lasting months to years after cessation. This means that even after prolonged abstinence, encountering drug-associated cues can trigger intense 'wanting' surges. The sensitized system is like a loaded spring: the individual may not crave in the abstract, but exposure to the right cue at the right moment can unleash compulsive motivation.

Q3: If 'wanting' and 'liking' are dissociable, what does this imply for the design of recovery interventions?

It implies that interventions targeting conscious pleasure or rational evaluation of drug effects will be insufficient. The 'wanting' system operates largely below conscious awareness and is triggered by Pavlovian cue associations. Effective interventions must either reduce cue-triggered 'wanting' (e.g., through extinction, cue exposure therapy, or pharmacological dampening of dopamine reactivity), or

strengthen competing 'wanting' for non-drug rewards (which is essentially what contingency management does). Simply understanding that the drug is no longer pleasurable does not extinguish the motivational pull.

2. George F. Koob

National Institute on Alcohol Abuse and Alcoholism (NIAAA), NIH; formerly Scripps Research Institute | Neuropharmacology / Behavioral Neuroscience

Semantic Frame: Allostatic Model — Opponent Process & Anti-Reward Systems

Seminal Work: Koob, G.F. & Le Moal, M. (2001). 'Drug addiction, dysregulation of reward, and allostasis.' Neuropsychopharmacology, 24(2), 97–129.

Synthesis: Koob's allostatic model reframes addiction as a spiraling dysregulation of brain reward and stress systems. Initial drug use activates reward circuits (the a-process), but counteradaptive opponent processes (the b-process) engage to restore homeostasis. With chronic stimulant use, these opponent processes—mediated by corticotropin-releasing factor (CRF), norepinephrine, and dynorphin in the extended amygdala—fail to return to baseline, establishing a new, dysphoric 'allostatic state.' The individual now uses drugs not for euphoria but to alleviate the persistent negative emotional state of withdrawal. This model explains the three-stage addiction cycle: binge/intoxication, withdrawal/negative affect, and preoccupation/anticipation.

Q1: What distinguishes the allostatic model from simple tolerance, and why does this distinction matter for amphetamine recovery?

Tolerance implies a simple reduction in drug effect that reverses with abstinence. Allostasis describes a permanent shift in the hedonic set point: the brain's reward thermostat has been recalibrated. For amphetamine users, this means that even after the acute withdrawal syndrome resolves, a protracted state of anhedonia, dysphoria, and stress hyperreactivity persists—sometimes for months. Recovery must address this 'reward deficit' state, not just the absence of acute withdrawal.

Q2: How does the recruitment of anti-reward stress systems specifically explain the relapse vulnerability of stimulant users?

Chronic amphetamine use sensitizes the brain's stress systems—particularly CRF circuits in the central nucleus of the amygdala—such that they remain hyperactive during abstinence. Any stressor can now trigger disproportionate negative affect, which the individual has learned can be temporarily relieved by drug use. This is negative reinforcement: using to escape misery, not to seek pleasure. The stress-sensitization persists well beyond acute withdrawal, creating a window of vulnerability that standard time-limited treatment programs often fail to cover.

Q3: If addiction represents an allostatic state rather than a homeostatic perturbation, can the reward system ever fully 'reset,' or is recovery inherently a managed condition?

The evidence is nuanced. Volkow's neuroimaging work shows partial recovery of dopamine transporter density after 12–17 months of methamphetamine abstinence, suggesting some neural recovery is possible. However, the allostatic framework predicts that the anti-reward systems may never fully normalize—the 'zero prime' set point may be permanently shifted. This supports conceptualizing recovery as an ongoing active process rather than a cure, much like managing hypertension. The individual can achieve stability, but the underlying vulnerability persists, requiring sustained environmental support and

behavioral strategies.

3. Nora D. Volkow

National Institute on Drug Abuse (NIDA), NIH | Psychiatry / Neuroimaging / Neuropharmacology

Semantic Frame: Dopaminergic Neuroimaging — Addiction as Impaired Self-Regulation

Seminal Work: Volkow, N.D. et al. (2001). 'Loss of dopamine transporters in methamphetamine abusers recovers with protracted abstinence.' Journal of Neuroscience, 21(23), 9414–9418. Also: Volkow, N.D. & Morales, M. (2015). 'The brain on drugs: From reward to addiction.' Cell, 162(2).

Synthesis: Volkow pioneered the use of PET imaging to demonstrate that stimulant addiction produces measurable, specific changes in the human dopamine system. Her work showed that methamphetamine abusers have significantly reduced striatal dopamine transporter (DAT) density, that this reduction partially recovers with protracted abstinence (12–17 months), and that dopamine changes in the prefrontal cortex impair the circuits responsible for self-control, salience attribution, and decision-making. Her overarching contribution has been to establish addiction as a brain disorder characterized by the interaction between a hypersensitized reward/motivation system and a compromised prefrontal control system.

Q1: What is the empirical evidence that the methamphetamine-damaged dopamine system can recover, and what are the limits of that recovery?

PET studies show that dopamine transporter levels in the striatum, which are significantly reduced in active methamphetamine users, show substantial recovery after 12–17 months of sustained abstinence—approaching but not fully reaching healthy control levels. However, brain metabolic activity, particularly in the thalamus, does not recover to the same extent. And neuropsychological function improves only partially, suggesting that DAT recovery is necessary but not sufficient for full cognitive restoration. The implication is hopeful but sobering: the brain can heal, but the healing is incomplete and slow.

Q2: How does the impairment of prefrontal cortical function create the phenomenology of 'loss of control' in stimulant addiction?

Stimulant-induced dopamine dysregulation specifically degrades the orbitofrontal cortex (which maintains attention to goals and monitors their value) and the anterior cingulate cortex (which mediates conflict monitoring and action selection). When these circuits are compromised, the individual cannot effectively override the motivational pull of drug cues, cannot adequately weigh long-term consequences against immediate gratification, and cannot flexibly shift attention away from drug-related stimuli. It is not a failure of willpower but a measurable deficit in the neural hardware of executive control.

Q3: How should this neuroimaging evidence reshape public policy and clinical practice around stimulant addiction?

It demolishes the moral model of addiction. When you can show a person their brain scan and demonstrate that the circuits for self-control are physically impaired, it reframes addiction from a character defect to a medical condition. Clinically, it argues for extended treatment durations—the brain needs 12+ months of abstinence for significant recovery—and for interventions that specifically support prefrontal function (cognitive remediation, exercise, structured decision-making environments). It also argues strongly against purely punitive approaches, which stress a system that is already in stress-mediated dysregulation.

4. Rita Z. Goldstein

Icahn School of Medicine at Mount Sinai, Department of Psychiatry | Neuropsychology / Cognitive Neuroscience

Semantic Frame: Impaired Response Inhibition and Salience Attribution (iRISA)

Seminal Work: Goldstein, R.Z. & Volkow, N.D. (2002). 'Drug addiction and its underlying neurobiological basis: Neuroimaging evidence for the involvement of the frontal cortex.' American Journal of Psychiatry, 159(10), 1642–1652. Updated (2011).

Synthesis: The iRISA model integrates two complementary deficits: hyperactive salience attribution to drug-related stimuli (driven by sensitized striatal-limbic circuits) and impaired inhibitory control over behavior (driven by hypoactive prefrontal cortex). Goldstein's contribution was to formalize this as a single neurocognitive syndrome with specific, testable neuroimaging signatures. In stimulant users, drug cues activate the ventral striatum and amygdala more strongly than in controls, while stop-signal and go/no-go tasks reveal diminished prefrontal activation. The model predicts that effective treatment must address both sides: dampen drug-cue salience and restore executive inhibition.

Q1: What makes the iRISA model a more complete account of stimulant addiction than either the incentive sensitization or habit models alone?

Incentive sensitization explains the motivational pull but not the failure to resist it. Habit models explain automaticity but not the intense subjective craving. iRISA integrates both: it posits that addiction is the conjunction of excessive salience (the push toward drug use) and deficient inhibition (the inability to pull back). This dual-deficit model better explains the clinical heterogeneity of stimulant addiction—some individuals are primarily 'salience-driven' while others are primarily 'control-impaired'—and suggests that treatment should be matched to the individual's neurocognitive profile.

Q2: How does the salience attribution component manifest specifically in amphetamine vs. other substance use disorders?

Amphetamines, more than most other substances, produce profound and lasting changes in dopamine-mediated salience processing. The sensitization of D1 receptor signaling in the nucleus accumbens makes amphetamine-related cues disproportionately salient—they literally capture attention more strongly than competing stimuli. Neuroimaging shows that this hyper-salience persists well into abstinence and correlates with relapse vulnerability. This is why environmental cue management is particularly critical for stimulant recovery.

Q3: What are the clinical implications of the inhibitory control deficit for treatment timing and modality selection?

The inhibitory control deficit suggests that purely insight-based therapies (psychodynamic, motivational) may be insufficient during early recovery, when prefrontal function is most impaired. Early treatment should emphasize structured environmental support, contingency management (which bypasses the need for self-regulation by providing external reinforcement), and potentially cognitive remediation or neuromodulation to rebuild executive function. As prefrontal recovery progresses—typically over months of abstinence—the individual becomes increasingly capable of benefiting from cognitive-behavioral and insight-oriented approaches.

5. Eric J. Nestler

Icahn School of Medicine at Mount Sinai, Friedman Brain Institute | Molecular Psychiatry / Epigenetics

Semantic Frame: Molecular & Epigenetic Neuroplasticity of Addiction

Seminal Work: Nestler, E.J. (2001). 'Molecular basis of long-term plasticity underlying addiction.' Nature Reviews Neuroscience, 2, 119–128. Also: 'Epigenetic mechanisms of drug addiction' (2014).

Synthesis: Nestler's work elucidated the molecular cascades through which repeated stimulant exposure produces lasting changes in gene expression, chromatin structure, and synaptic morphology in the mesolimbic reward circuit. His research on the transcription factor DeltaFosB demonstrated that this protein accumulates in the nucleus accumbens with chronic drug use, persists for weeks after cessation, and directly modifies the expression of genes involved in synaptic plasticity and reward sensitivity. This provided the first clear molecular mechanism for the long-lasting nature of addiction vulnerability.

Q1: What role does DeltaFosB play as a molecular switch in the transition from recreational stimulant use to addiction?

DeltaFosB is an unusually stable transcription factor that accumulates gradually with repeated drug exposure and, once present, alters the expression of downstream genes that increase sensitivity to drug rewards and enhance the motivation to seek them. It is essentially a molecular memory of chronic drug exposure—a switch that, once thrown, is slow to reset. In the context of amphetamines, the dopamine surge produced by each use drives DeltaFosB accumulation, progressively rewriting the transcriptional landscape of accumbens neurons to favor drug-seeking behavior.

Q2: How do epigenetic modifications extend the timeline of addiction vulnerability beyond what pharmacological models alone predict?

Epigenetic changes—histone acetylation, DNA methylation, chromatin remodeling—can persist for months or years after the last drug exposure, long after the drug itself has cleared the system. These modifications do not change the DNA sequence but alter which genes are accessible for transcription. This explains why addiction vulnerability persists well beyond the pharmacokinetic half-life of the drug: the chromatin landscape of reward neurons has been physically restructured. It also opens new therapeutic avenues through histone deacetylase inhibitors and other epigenetic modulators.

Q3: What does molecular-level evidence suggest about the theoretical possibility of 'curing' versus 'managing' stimulant addiction?

The molecular evidence paints a picture of deep, durable neural change. While some modifications reverse with abstinence, others—particularly epigenetic changes and structural remodeling of dendritic spines—persist indefinitely. This does not mean recovery is impossible, but it does mean that the recovered brain is not identical to the pre-addiction brain. Recovery involves building new circuits and behaviors on top of altered architecture, rather than restoring a pristine original state. This is why environmental restructuring and ongoing behavioral support matter so much—they provide the scaffolding that compensates for permanently altered substrates.

CLUSTER B: LEARNING, HABIT & COMPUTATIONAL FRAMES

6. Barry J. Everitt

University of Cambridge, Department of Psychology (Emeritus) | Behavioral Neuroscience / Learning Theory

Semantic Frame: Habit-Compulsion Transition

Seminal Work: Everitt, B.J. & Robbins, T.W. (2005). 'Neural systems of reinforcement for drug addiction: From actions to habits to compulsion.' Nature Neuroscience, 8(11), 1481–1489.

Synthesis: Everitt and Robbins proposed that drug addiction represents a transition from voluntary, goal-directed drug use to habitual and ultimately compulsive drug seeking. This transition is neurally underpinned by a shift from prefrontal cortical to striatal control over behavior, and from ventral to dorsal striatal dominance—mediated by the serial connectivity of these regions through midbrain dopamine projections. Using sophisticated second-order reinforcement schedules in rats, they demonstrated that well-established cocaine-seeking behavior becomes resistant to outcome devaluation (a hallmark of habit) and depends on dorsolateral striatal dopamine rather than ventral striatal circuitry.

Q1: How does the actions-to-habits-to-compulsions framework explain why stimulant users persist in drug seeking even when they report no longer enjoying the drug?

Once drug seeking has become habitual—stamped in as a stimulus-response association in the dorsal striatum—it no longer requires the drug's hedonic value to sustain it. The behavior is triggered automatically by environmental cues, just as a person might find themselves walking to their office on a weekend morning out of habit. But habit alone is not compulsion. Compulsion emerges when habitual drug seeking is combined with impaired prefrontal 'top-down' control—the individual cannot override the automatic behavior even when they recognize its harmful consequences.

Q2: What distinguishes the habit model from the incentive sensitization model, and are they truly competing accounts?

They are complementary rather than competing. Incentive sensitization explains the motivational 'push'—the cue-triggered wanting that initiates drug seeking. The habit model explains the automaticity of the behavioral response once initiated. In practice, both processes operate in the same individual: sensitized wanting triggers the urge, and habitual motor programs execute the drug-seeking sequence without deliberate intention. The clinical implication is that treatment must address both the motivational and the habitual components.

Q3: What does this model imply for the optimal timing and sequencing of recovery interventions?

The model suggests that early recovery, when drug-seeking habits are strongest and prefrontal control is most impaired, should prioritize environmental restructuring (removing cues that trigger habitual sequences) and contingency management (providing alternative reinforcement for non-drug behavior). As prefrontal function recovers with sustained abstinence, the individual becomes increasingly capable of deploying goal-directed strategies—cognitive behavioral techniques, values-based decision making—that can compete with and eventually override habitual drug-seeking patterns.

7. Trevor W. Robbins

University of Cambridge, Department of Psychology | Cognitive Neuroscience / Neuropsychopharmacology

Semantic Frame: Impulsivity-Compulsivity Endophenotype Spectrum

Seminal Work: Robbins, T.W., Gillan, C.M., Smith, D.G., de Wit, S. & Ersche, K.D. (2012). 'Neurocognitive endophenotypes of impulsivity and compulsivity.' Current Opinion in Neurobiology, 22(6).

Synthesis: Robbins extended the habit-compulsion framework to incorporate individual vulnerability factors, demonstrating that pre-existing traits of impulsivity and compulsivity—measurable through neurocognitive tasks and associated with distinct neural signatures—serve as endophenotypes for stimulant addiction. Working with Karen Ersche and others, his group showed that unaffected siblings of stimulant-dependent individuals share neurocognitive impairments in response inhibition and increased gray matter in the putamen, suggesting that these traits precede and predispose to addiction rather than merely resulting from drug exposure.

Q1: What evidence distinguishes pre-existing neurocognitive vulnerability from drug-induced impairment in stimulant addiction?

The sibling studies are decisive. Unaffected biological siblings of cocaine/amphetamine-dependent individuals show the same pattern of impaired stop-signal inhibition and abnormal fronto-striatal structure as their addicted siblings—despite never having developed addiction themselves. This demonstrates that the neurocognitive profile is not merely a consequence of drug exposure but a pre-existing vulnerability trait that is shared within families, presumably through genetic and/or early environmental pathways.

Q2: How does the impulsivity-compulsivity dimension map onto the stages of stimulant addiction?

High trait impulsivity facilitates initial experimentation and early escalation—impulsive individuals are more likely to try drugs, more likely to take higher doses, and less able to regulate intake. As addiction progresses, compulsivity traits become more relevant: the individual develops rigid, stereotyped drug-seeking patterns that persist despite punishment and resist flexible updating. This dimensional mapping suggests that impulsivity-focused interventions (delay of gratification training, working memory enhancement) may be most relevant in early stages, while compulsivity-focused interventions (habit-breaking, exposure-response prevention) become more critical later.

Q3: What are the implications of endophenotype research for prevention and early identification of stimulant addiction risk?

If we can identify neurocognitive profiles that predict addiction vulnerability before drug exposure occurs, we can target prevention efforts more precisely. A young person showing high trait impulsivity, poor response inhibition, and a family history of stimulant use disorder could be offered targeted interventions—executive function training, environmental enrichment, mentoring—before addiction develops. This shifts the paradigm from reactive treatment to proactive prevention, grounded in measurable neurobehavioral risk markers.

8. A. David Redish

University of Minnesota, Department of Neuroscience | Computational Neuroscience

Semantic Frame: Computational Reinforcement Learning Model of Addiction

Seminal Work: Redish, A.D. (2004). 'Addiction as a computational process gone awry.' Science, 306(5703), 1944–1947.

Synthesis: Redish formalized the computational account of addiction as a disorder of the reinforcement learning system. Drawing on temporal difference (TD) learning models, he demonstrated how addictive drugs produce aberrant prediction error signals—pharmacologically amplified dopamine surges that overwrite the normal updating of reward values. Drugs effectively 'hack' the reward prediction error signal to teach the brain that drugs are far more valuable than they actually are, and this inflated value persists because the pharmacological override bypasses the normal correction mechanisms that would eventually diminish value through satiation or negative consequences.

Q1: How does the pharmacological 'hijacking' of prediction error signals differ qualitatively from the normal process of learning to like a natural reward?

Natural rewards produce a prediction error that diminishes as the reward becomes expected—this is how the system learns accurate values and prevents over-pursuit of any single reward. Addictive drugs bypass this self-correcting mechanism by directly activating dopamine release independent of actual outcome evaluation. Each dose produces a fresh, pharmacologically-driven prediction error signal regardless of how many times the drug has been taken. The system never 'learns' the drug's true value because the teaching signal is being artificially generated rather than computed from experience.

Q2: What does this computational account predict about the specific failure modes of decision-making in stimulant addiction?

It predicts several specific deficits: over-valuation of drug rewards (inflated learned values), under-valuation of non-drug alternatives (because normal prediction errors are comparatively weak), failure to update drug value downward in response to negative consequences (because the pharmacological override overwhelms negative prediction errors), and temporal myopia (because the immediate pharmacological signal dominates delayed consequence signals). Each of these predictions has been confirmed in behavioral studies with stimulant-dependent populations.

Q3: How might computational models inform the design of 'precision' recovery interventions for stimulant addiction?

If we can computationally fingerprint an individual's specific pattern of value distortion—whether they primarily over-value drugs, under-value alternatives, fail to learn from punishment, or discount the future excessively—we can match interventions to their specific computational deficit. Someone with primarily inflated drug valuation might benefit most from extinction-based approaches; someone with suppressed alternative valuation might benefit most from behavioral activation and contingency management; someone with temporal discounting might benefit from episodic future thinking interventions. The computational framework provides the diagnostic taxonomy for personalized treatment.

9. Warren K. Bickel

Virginia Tech Carilion Research Institute, Fralin Biomedical Research Institute | Behavioral Economics / Neuroeconomics

Semantic Frame: Temporal Discounting & Competing Neurobehavioral Decision Systems

Seminal Work: Bickel, W.K. et al. (2007). 'Behavioral and neuroeconomics of drug addiction.' Current Drug Abuse Reviews, 1(1). Also: Bickel, W.K. et al. (2014). 'The behavioral economics and neuroeconomics of

reinforcer pathologies.' Annual Review of Clinical Psychology, 10.

Synthesis: Bickel established temporal discounting—the tendency to prefer smaller immediate rewards over larger delayed ones—as a trans-diagnostic marker of addiction. His Competing Neurobehavioral Decision Systems (CNDS) framework proposes that addiction reflects a dominance of the impulsive (limbic) system over the executive (prefrontal) system in intertemporal choice. For stimulant users, this manifests as extraordinarily steep discounting of future rewards, meaning the immediate dopamine hit from amphetamine use massively outweighs the perceived value of any future benefit from abstinence.

Q1: How does temporal discounting function as a mechanism rather than merely a correlate of stimulant addiction?

Temporal discounting is both a vulnerability factor and a consequence of drug use, creating a vicious cycle. Pre-existing steep discounting predisposes individuals to choose immediate drug rewards over delayed sobriety benefits. But chronic stimulant use further impairs prefrontal executive function, steepening discounting further. This creates a trap: the very drug that exploits the discounting bias also worsens it, progressively narrowing the temporal window within which consequences can influence behavior. Interventions that flatten the discounting curve—such as episodic future thinking, where individuals vividly imagine future positive outcomes—can break this cycle.

Q2: What does the Competing Neurobehavioral Decision Systems framework predict about when and why stimulant users will relapse?

The CNDS framework predicts that relapse is most likely when the executive system is temporarily depleted or offline—during stress, cognitive fatigue, hunger, or sleep deprivation—and the impulsive system encounters drug cues in the absence of executive oversight. This explains the classic relapse scenarios: the person in early recovery who encounters a former using partner late at night when tired and stressed. The framework prescribes that recovery environments should minimize these vulnerability states and provide contingency management during the windows when executive function is lowest.

Q3: How does behavioral economics bridge the gap between neuroscience and policy in stimulant addiction treatment?

Behavioral economics provides the theoretical justification for contingency management in a language that policymakers understand: incentive design. If addiction is fundamentally a choice disorder driven by steep temporal discounting, then the remedy is to restructure the choice architecture—make the immediate rewards for abstinence tangible and competitive with the immediate rewards of drug use. This is precisely what contingency management does. The behavioral economic framework also explains why punishment-based approaches fail: they add delayed negative consequences to a population that, by definition, is insensitive to delayed consequences.

10. Karen D. Ersche

University of Cambridge, Department of Psychiatry | Neuropsychology / Addiction Neuroscience

Semantic Frame: Stimulant Addiction Endophenotype

Seminal Work: Ersche, K.D. et al. (2012). 'Abnormal brain structure implicated in stimulant drug addiction.' Science, 335(6068), 601–604.

Synthesis: Ersche demonstrated that both stimulant-dependent individuals and their unaffected biological siblings share structural brain abnormalities—enlarged putamen, reduced inferior frontal gyrus volume, and impaired white matter integrity—that predispose to addiction. This landmark study in Science established that key neural features of stimulant addiction are not simply consequences of drug exposure but pre-existing endophenotypes. Her computational modeling work has further shown that stimulant users exhibit reduced punishment learning rates and increased behavioral 'stickiness,' suggesting specific algorithmic deficits in flexible decision-making.

Q1: What does the finding of shared brain abnormalities in unaffected siblings fundamentally change about how we understand the etiology of stimulant addiction?

It dismantles the assumption that brain differences in addiction are purely drug-induced damage. The siblings study reveals that the brains of individuals who go on to develop stimulant addiction were already different before they ever used drugs. This shifts the etiological narrative from 'drugs cause brain disease' to 'pre-existing brain variation creates vulnerability that drugs exploit.' The practical implication is enormous: it means prevention can be targeted at individuals with identifiable risk profiles, and treatment can be designed to compensate for specific pre-existing deficits rather than solely repairing drug-induced damage.

Q2: How does the concept of reduced punishment sensitivity manifest in the real-world behavior of amphetamine users?

Reduced punishment sensitivity means that negative consequences—job loss, relationship breakdown, legal trouble—have less impact on behavioral updating than they would in non-vulnerable individuals. 11. Stephen T. Higgins

Synthesis: Higgins developed and validated the voucher-based reinforcement therapy (VBRT) approach, demonstrating that providing escalating monetary vouchers contingent on drug-negative urine samples dramatically improved cocaine/stimulant abstinence rates compared to standard treatment. Q1: Why does contingency management work better for stimulant addiction than for most other substance use disorders, and better than any other behavioral therapy?

Stimulant addiction is characterized by a profound devaluation of non-drug reinforcers. Contingency management works by creating an artificial but tangible competing reinforcer—money, prizes—that can compete with drug rewards in the immediate temporal window that matters to the stimulant user's steeply discounting decision system. It does not require intact executive function, insight, or motivation to work—it operates at the level of operant reinforcement, which is precisely the level at which stimulant addiction is maintained.

Three barriers dominate. Each barrier requires a different strategy: education for the first, health-economic modeling for the second, and policy advocacy for the third.

This is the central critique, and the evidence is honestly mixed. Most studies show that the abstinence gains during active contingency management partially erode after incentives cease. Q1: What makes social identity transition a more powerful predictor of sustained recovery than clinical treatment variables?

Clinical treatment addresses symptoms, skills, and neurobiological function. But identity addresses the answer to the question 'Who am I?' If the individual's primary social identity remains 'amphetamine user,' then every clinical gain exists within a framework that ultimately points back toward use. When the individual begins to identify primarily as 'a person in recovery,' 'a good parent,' 'a student,' 'a professional'—identities that are incompatible with active drug use—recovery becomes self-reinforcing because drug use threatens something the person values more than the drug: their sense of who they are. This is why mutual aid groups and recovery communities produce effects that extend well beyond their specific program content.

Q2: How does the SIMOR model explain the particular challenge of identity transition for methamphetamine users?

Methamphetamine often becomes deeply intertwined with identity in ways that other drugs may not. In certain communities, it is associated with sexual identity (e.g., chemsex/party and play culture), subcultural belonging, or economic survival networks. Leaving methamphetamine use behind may therefore require not just giving up a substance but disconnecting from an entire social world and reconstructing one's identity from scratch. The SIMOR model predicts that the availability of alternative identity-congruent social groups—recovery communities, vocational programs, peer support networks—is a critical determinant of recovery success for this population.

Q3: How should recovery services be redesigned to facilitate social identity transition rather than merely treating symptoms?

Services should be designed as community-building platforms, not just clinical delivery systems. This means investing in peer recovery support, creating structured opportunities for individuals to develop non-drug social identities (vocational training, volunteer work, recreation, education), and recognizing that the quality and number of an individual's pro-recovery group memberships is a primary outcome metric—not just abstinence. Recovery housing, mutual aid groups, recovery community organizations, and alumni networks are not afterthoughts to treatment; they are the primary medium through which durable recovery occurs.

IV. GAPS, TENSIONS & FUTURE DIRECTIONS

Unresolved Tensions. The field's most productive tensions remain unresolved and generative. The debate between habit theorists (Everitt & Robbins) and incentive sensitization theorists (Berridge & Robinson) about whether compulsive drug seeking is fundamentally automatic or motivationally driven continues to refine both accounts. The tension between the brain disease model (Volkow, Koob) and the social/ecological models (Best, White, Maté) reflects a genuine ontological question about whether addiction is best understood at the neural, psychological, or social level of analysis—or whether it requires a multi-level account that has not yet been fully articulated.

Critical Gaps. Several important gaps persist in the amphetamine recovery landscape. First, the pharmacotherapy gap: with no FDA-approved medication, stimulant addiction remains the largest unmet need in addiction medicine. Second, the implementation gap: contingency management, the most effective behavioral treatment, reaches fewer than 10% of eligible patients. Third, the precision gap: computational models can now characterize individual differences in value-based decision-making, but no system yet translates these computational fingerprints into personalized treatment recommendations. Fourth, the integration gap: each of the 22 experts profiled here operates within a relatively siloed literature, and no overarching framework yet integrates molecular mechanisms, computational processes, clinical interventions, and social determinants into a coherent model of stimulant addiction and recovery.

Future Directions. The frontier is converging on several promising trajectories. Adaptive, multi-component treatment platforms that sequence and combine interventions based on real-time assessment of individual neurocognitive and psychosocial status. Digital therapeutics that operationalize contingency management, mindfulness training, and CBT at scale through smartphone-delivered interventions with remote biomarker monitoring. Neuromodulation-pharmacotherapy combinations that target specific circuit dysfunctions identified through individual brain imaging. And recovery ecosystem engineering that applies recovery capital and self-determination theory principles to design communities and policies that make recovery the path of least resistance rather than an act of heroic willpower.

The Ontological Opportunity. For those building recovery-oriented systems—particularly ontology-driven personal operating systems rooted in cognitive science—this report maps the conceptual terrain that such a system must navigate. The 22 semantic frames identified here represent the fundamental concepts, relations, and intervention targets that a recovery ontology must encode. The topology of their relationships—which frames complement, compete with, or subsume one another—provides the structural blueprint for an inference engine that can reason about individual recovery trajectories. The three abstracted questions per frame and their author-aligned responses provide seed material for the kind of domain-expert knowledge that such a system must internalize.
