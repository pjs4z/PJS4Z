# Executive function recovery after amphetamines in ADHD: A multi-disciplinary frontier map

**The most important finding across every discipline researched is this: amphetamine-class stimulants improve executive function in ADHD only while pharmacologically active — benefits are state-dependent and do not persist after discontinuation.** This consensus, converged upon by neuroimagers, pharmacologists, computational modelers, and clinicians alike, carries a profound implication for anyone building recovery programs: "recovery" after amphetamine use is not about restoring something that was built during treatment, but about constructing an entirely new multi-system scaffold — motivational, metabolic, circadian, metacognitive, and environmental — that the medication never provided. The field is at an inflection point where at least 23 frontier researchers, working from radically different conceptual frames, are converging on complementary pieces of what such a scaffold would look like. This report maps those researchers, their frames, and the actionable intelligence their work generates.

---

## Part I: State of the frontier

### Stimulant benefits vanish upon discontinuation — but the picture is more nuanced than it appears

Randomized withdrawal studies consistently demonstrate that executive function returns to pre-medication baseline when amphetamine-class stimulants are stopped. In lisdexamfetamine withdrawal trials, **67.5%** of adults met criteria for "treatment failure" within a median of just 17 days after switching to placebo. The landmark MTA study showed that the selective benefit of medication assignment was no longer detectable at three years. A 2024 meta-analysis in *Neuroscience and Biobehavioral Reviews* confirmed that chronic methylphenidate and atomoxetine produce comparable improvements in executive function (Hedges' g = 0.34–0.59) — but only during active treatment. Critically, there is **no evidence of a "worse-than-baseline" rebound**: discontinuation at therapeutic doses produces transient fatigue and mood changes lasting one to three days, not classical withdrawal.

However, the "return to baseline" narrative conceals important complexities. Katya Rubia's neuroimaging work raises the concern that chronic stimulant use may upregulate striatal dopamine transporters, potentially leaving the brain in a slightly worse neurochemical position than before treatment. Michael Frank's computational models suggest that learning-dependent effects — corticostriatal synaptic plasticity accumulated during treatment — may partially persist, even if the acute pharmacological effects do not. And Sandra Kooij's circadian research reveals that stimulants compound the already-delayed circadian rhythms of ADHD adults, creating a "hidden sleep debt" whose cognitive toll masquerades as executive dysfunction.

### The concurrent optimization landscape has matured considerably

The 2025 Lancet Psychiatry component network meta-analysis of 113 RCTs (14,887 adults) confirmed stimulants as the only intervention with robust short-term efficacy for core ADHD symptoms. But the most exciting development is the growing evidence base for multimodal optimization. Physical exercise now commands the highest effect size among non-pharmacological interventions (**d = 0.93** in meta-meta-analyses), with cognitively engaging exercise roughly doubling the effect of simple aerobic activity. Mindfulness achieved a clinician-rated symptom reduction effect of SMD = –0.79 in the same Lancet analysis. CBT specifically targeting executive function skills (time management, organization, planning) shows clinically meaningful benefits even in already-medicated patients. Chronotherapy — timed melatonin plus morning bright light — reduced ADHD symptoms by 14% in randomized trials. And broad-spectrum micronutrient supplementation doubled ADHD symptom improvement versus placebo in Julia Rucklidge's adult RCT. The convergence of these findings points decisively toward an integrated "operating system" approach.

### Where disciplines converge — and where they productively diverge

Every discipline converges on one core insight: **executive function is not a single internal module that medication toggles on and off, but a multi-system phenomenon requiring neurochemical, metabolic, circadian, metacognitive, motivational, and environmental support.** Every discipline also agrees that dopaminergic signaling sits at the center of the ADHD executive function story.

The productive divergences concern level of explanation and intervention target. Pharmacologists (Arnsten) operate at the molecular-synaptic level, emphasizing catecholamine optimization of prefrontal cortex circuits. Computational neuroscientists (Frank, Hauser) model ADHD as aberrant dopamine-dependent gating and neural gain. Network neuroscientists (Castellanos) see disrupted dynamics between the default mode network and task-positive networks. Ecological psychologists (Rietveld, Chemero) reject brain-centric accounts entirely, locating executive function in agent-environment coupling dynamics. Motivation scientists (Scheres) argue that stimulants may paradoxically undermine the intrinsic motivation that sustains self-regulation. And phenomenologists (de Haan) reframe the entire endeavor as a question about sense-making, not circuit repair.

The biggest open question — and the single largest gap in the literature — is the absence of well-designed prospective studies tracking executive function after amphetamine discontinuation specifically in adults. Most discontinuation research focuses on symptom relapse in children. For a startup building adult recovery programs, this represents both a knowledge gap and a competitive opportunity: whoever generates the first rigorous longitudinal adult discontinuation data will own the evidence base.

## Part II: Topology of semantic frames

The 23 experts profiled in this report can be organized into a conceptual topology showing how their frames relate, overlap, and diverge. Imagine this as a map with three concentric rings and several lateral bridges.

### The inner ring: neural mechanism

At the center sit the researchers who work at the level of neurons, synapses, and neurotransmitters. **Amy Arnsten** occupies the molecular core, modeling executive function as an inverted-U dose-response relationship between catecholamines and prefrontal cortex network connectivity. **Michael Frank** extends this upward into computational territory, modeling dopamine as a reinforcement learning signal that gates information into and out of working memory. **Tobias Hauser** abstracts further, framing ADHD as impaired neural gain — the amplification factor at synapses — that produces behavioral variability across domains. These three share a common language (dopamine, prefrontal cortex, computational parameters) but operate at progressively higher levels of abstraction.

### The middle ring: systems and networks

Surrounding the molecular core are the systems-level thinkers. **F. Xavier Castellanos** maps ADHD as disrupted dynamics between the default mode network and task-positive networks. **Edmund Sonuga-Barke** extends this into a multi-pathway dynamical systems model where executive dysfunction, delay aversion, and temporal processing are semi-independent pathways. **Katya Rubia** bridges inward toward the molecular ring with her neuroimaging work on right inferior frontal cortex activation and outward toward clinical translation with her pursuit of neurofeedback alternatives. **Ryan Smith** connects the computational ring to the systems ring through predictive processing theory, framing attention as precision-weighting of prediction errors across the cortical hierarchy.

### The outer ring: person-in-context

The outermost ring contains the researchers who fundamentally reject brain-centric explanations. **Erik Rietveld** and **Anthony Chemero** locate executive function in agent-environment dynamics — affordance landscapes and dynamical coupling, respectively. **Sanneke de Haan** frames psychiatric conditions as disorders of sense-making across four integrated dimensions (experiential, neurophysiological, socio-cultural, existential). **Neil Levy** extends the cognitive system outward through the extended mind thesis, arguing that medication is one cognitive scaffold among many.

### Lateral bridges: the intervention specialists

Cutting across all three rings are the applied researchers whose work integrates multiple levels. **Russell Barkley** bridges theory to clinical reality with his executive-function-as-self-regulation model. **Thomas Brown** (deceased 2025) bridged assessment to clinical practice with his six-cluster model of executive dysfunction. **Craig Surman** bridges pharmacology to functional outcomes, documenting the "treatment gap" between medication and real-world executive performance. **Mary Solanto** bridges neuroscience to skills-building through her Meta-Cognitive Therapy. **Lidia Zylowska** bridges contemplative practice to neuroscience through her ADHD-adapted mindfulness programs. **Sandra Kooij** bridges chronobiology to clinical psychiatry through chronotherapy. **Adina Maeir** bridges occupational science to cognitive rehabilitation through her Cog-Fun approach. **Julia Rucklidge** bridges nutritional science to psychiatric treatment through broad-spectrum micronutrient research. **John Ratey** and **Betsy Hoza** bridge exercise science to executive function neuroscience. **Anouk Scheres** bridges self-determination theory to ADHD motivation research. **Charlotte Cecil** bridges molecular epigenetics to developmental psychopathology.

The critical insight this topology reveals is that **no single ring is sufficient**. Molecular-level interventions (medication) operate within the inner ring but leave the middle and outer rings unaddressed. Recovery programs must scaffold across all three rings simultaneously.

## Part III: Expert profiles

### 1. Amy F.T. Arnsten

**Affiliation:** Professor of Neurobiology, Yale University School of Medicine | **Discipline:** Neuropharmacology

**Semantic frame — The inverted-U catecholamine optimizer:** Arnsten's core insight is that the prefrontal cortex operates within a narrow "Goldilocks zone" of catecholamine signaling. Norepinephrine acting at α2A-adrenoceptors strengthens PFC network signals; dopamine at D1 receptors reduces noise. Either too little or too much of either neurotransmitter impairs executive function. ADHD reflects suboptimal catecholamine modulation; stimulants restore it — but only at the correct dose.

**Seminal work:** "Stress Signalling Pathways That Impair Prefrontal Cortex Structure and Function" (2009, *Nature Reviews Neuroscience*); development of guanfacine (Intuniv) for ADHD based on her α2A-adrenoceptor research.

**Q1 — How do amphetamines improve executive function mechanistically?** At therapeutic doses, amphetamine preferentially increases NE and DA in the PFC. NE strengthens network connectivity via α2A receptors (closing HCN channels); DA sculpts inputs by weakening irrelevant connections at D1 receptors. This restores the signal-to-noise ratio in PFC circuits.

**Q2 — What happens to PFC function upon discontinuation?** PFC catecholamine levels return to their genetically determined suboptimal state. Network connectivity weakens. However, structural PFC maturation and environmental enrichment may partially offset the decline. The critical risk is that stress during discontinuation triggers α1 and excessive D1 signaling, actively impairing PFC function beyond baseline.

**Q3 — How should executive function be optimized during treatment?** Stay on the ascending limb of the inverted-U curve through careful dose titration. Manage stress aggressively, as stress rapidly takes PFC offline via protein kinase C signaling. Combine stimulants with α2A-agonists (guanfacine) for complementary network strengthening. Prioritize sleep, which affects baseline catecholamine dynamics.

### 2. Russell A. Barkley

**Affiliation:** Clinical Professor of Psychiatry, Medical University of South Carolina | **Discipline:** Neuropsychology / Clinical Psychology

**Semantic frame — Executive function as self-regulation, and ADHD as its chronic failure:** Barkley's unified theory positions ADHD as fundamentally a deficit in behavioral inhibition that cascades into impairments across working memory, emotion regulation, internalized speech, and reconstitution. Executive functions are forms of self-directed action, and ADHD is a performance deficit — knowing what to do but being unable to do it at the point of performance.

**Seminal work:** "Behavioral Inhibition, Sustained Attention, and Executive Functions: Constructing a Unifying Theory of ADHD" (1997, *Psychological Bulletin*); *Executive Functions* (2012, Guilford Press).

**Q1 — Are stimulant benefits enduring?** No. Stimulants optimize neurochemistry temporarily but do not produce lasting changes to EF architecture. The MTA study confirms that benefits attenuate over years and are not maintained after discontinuation. Medication is an ongoing prosthetic, not a cure.

**Q2 — Why do neuropsych tests miss ADHD's EF deficits?** Testing environments provide the very structure ADHD individuals lack internally. Real-world EF requires sustained self-directed management of complex, unstructured tasks — exactly what tests eliminate. Rating scales measuring daily functioning are more ecologically valid.

**Q3 — What does "recovery" mean for ADHD?** "Recovery" is a misleading frame. ADHD is a chronic neurodevelopmental condition. The goal is not recovery but optimization of prosthetic supports — medication, environmental structure, behavioral strategies — that bridge the gap between EF capacity and real-world demands.

### 3. Katya Rubia

**Affiliation:** Professor of Cognitive Neuroscience, King's College London | **Discipline:** Cognitive Neuroscience / Neuroimaging

**Semantic frame — Dissociated brain networks and the concern about neurochemical adaptation:** Rubia maps specific brain network dysfunctions to specific cognitive deficits, demonstrating that stimulants most consistently upregulate the right inferior frontal cortex. Her critical contribution is the concern that long-term stimulant use may upregulate striatal dopamine transporters, potentially leaving the brain **worse off** after discontinuation than before treatment began.

**Seminal work:** "Effects of Stimulants on Brain Function in ADHD: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis" (2014, *Biological Psychiatry*); chronic medication effects meta-analysis (2024, *Neuroscience and Biobehavioral Reviews*).

**Q1 — What happens to brain function upon discontinuation?** Brain activation patterns likely revert to pre-medication underactivation. The concerning finding is that DAT upregulation from chronic stimulant use may produce a rebound effect — the brain clearing dopamine faster than baseline. Drug holidays may prevent full neurochemical adaptation.

**Q2 — Can neurotherapeutics produce lasting changes drugs cannot?** fMRI neurofeedback and transcranial stimulation targeting right IFC may induce neuroplastic changes that persist without ongoing intervention. This is the most promising frontier for durable executive function improvement.

**Q3 — How do stimulants and non-stimulants compare?** Her 2024 meta-analysis shows chronic methylphenidate and atomoxetine produce comparable executive function improvements with similar effect sizes, because both enhance catecholamine signaling in PFC through different mechanisms.

### 4. Thomas E. Brown (1942–2025)

**Affiliation:** Clinical Professor of Psychiatry & Neuroscience, UC Riverside School of Medicine (final appointment) | **Discipline:** Clinical Neuropsychological Assessment

**Semantic frame — The six-cluster model and the emotional core of executive dysfunction:** Brown reconceptualized ADHD as impairment in the brain's management system across six clusters: Activation, Focus, Effort, Emotion, Memory, and Action. His crucial innovation was centering emotion as intrinsic to executive dysfunction — not a comorbidity — and designing assessment tools (Brown EF/A Scales) that capture real-world EF deficits that traditional tests miss.

**Seminal work:** *A New Understanding of ADHD in Children and Adults: Executive Function Impairments* (2013, Routledge); Brown Executive Function/Attention Scales.

**Q1 — Why do standard neuropsych tests fail high-IQ ADHD adults?** Tests measure narrow, moment-in-time operations in structured environments. Real-world EF requires sustained self-management of complex, unstructured tasks. **73%** of high-IQ adults with ADHD show significant EF impairment that testing environments mask.

**Q2 — How should medication effects on EF be measured?** Through multi-domain scales that assess the full breadth of EF impairment independently from DSM symptom reduction, because the two can dissociate.

**Q3 — What role does emotion play?** Emotional dysregulation is central to EF impairment. Emotions "take over" thinking like a computer virus hijacking a system. Stimulants improve emotional regulation by optimizing PFC modulation of limbic responses. When discontinued, emotional flooding returns — often the most distressing aspect of withdrawal.

### 5. Craig B.H. Surman

**Affiliation:** Associate Professor of Psychiatry, Harvard Medical School; Scientific Coordinator, Adult ADHD Research Program, Massachusetts General Hospital | **Discipline:** Clinical Psychiatry / Adult ADHD Treatment

**Semantic frame — The treatment gap between symptom reduction and functional recovery:** Surman systematically evaluates whether ADHD treatments actually improve executive functioning and emotional behavior beyond core DSM symptoms. His core finding: even optimally medicated adults continue to struggle with executive functioning, revealing a significant treatment gap.

**Seminal work:** "Do ADHD Treatments Improve Executive Behavior Beyond Core ADHD Symptoms?" (2023, *J Clinical Pharmacology*); "Treating Executive Function in Youth With ADHD" (2024, *J Attention Disorders*) — 136-study systematic review.

**Q1 — Is there a treatment gap?** Yes. Stimulants produce 65–144% proportional improvement in behavioral EF measures relative to core symptom improvement, but many treated adults still struggle. Combination therapy — stimulant plus non-stimulant, or medication plus CBT — is needed to close the gap.

**Q2 — What happens to EF and emotion regulation upon discontinuation?** Improvements in both executive behavior and emotional regulation are medication-dependent. Loss of emotional regulation upon discontinuation can be functionally devastating. Behavioral supports should be strengthened before, during, and after any medication changes.

**Q3 — How should clinicians optimize EF during treatment?** Multimodal approach: optimize pharmacotherapy (titrate for EF, not just symptoms), add CBT targeting EF deficits, assess EF independently from core symptoms, consider combination pharmacotherapy, and explicitly monitor emotional regulation.

### 6. Michael J. Frank

**Affiliation:** Professor and Director, Center for Computational Brain Science, Brown University | **Discipline:** Computational Cognitive Neuroscience

**Semantic frame — Dopaminergic gating of working memory and the economics of cognitive effort:** Frank models executive function as a reinforcement learning system where dopamine trains the basal ganglia to "gate" information into and out of prefrontal working memory. His landmark 2020 *Science* paper showed methylphenidate specifically amplifies the perceived **benefits** of cognitive effort (not reduces costs), directly measured with PET imaging.

**Seminal work:** "Dopamine promotes cognitive effort by biasing the benefits versus costs of cognitive work" (2020, *Science*); "Testing Computational Models of Dopamine and Noradrenaline Dysfunction in ADHD" (2007, *Neuropsychopharmacology*).

**Q1 — How does dopamine support executive function in ADHD?** Dopamine serves dual roles: in the striatum, phasic dopamine trains Go/NoGo pathways for working memory gating; in the PFC, it modulates representation stability. Stimulants boost both, and critically increase willingness to exert cognitive effort by amplifying perceived benefits.

**Q2 — What are the computational consequences of discontinuation?** Reduced Go-pathway excitability impairs WM updating; reduced benefit-to-cost ratio decreases motivation for cognitive effort; reinforcement learning slows. However, learned gating strategies (corticostriatal synaptic weights) may partially persist as trained computational habits.

**Q3 — Can models distinguish reversible from lasting effects?** Yes — his framework distinguishes online dopamine effects (acute modulation) from learning-dependent effects (accumulated synaptic plasticity). Chronic stimulant use could train better gating strategies that partially survive discontinuation, but the dynamic range of adaptation would be reduced.

### 7. F. Xavier Castellanos

**Affiliation:** Professor Emeritus, Child and Adolescent Psychiatry, NYU Grossman School of Medicine; Nathan Kline Institute | **Discipline:** Network Neuroscience / Systems Neuroscience

**Semantic frame — Default mode interference and network switching dynamics:** Castellanos shifted the field from a prefrontal-striatal deficit model to a systems neuroscience model where ADHD reflects disrupted interactions between the default mode network and task-positive networks. His "Default Mode Interference Hypothesis" (with Sonuga-Barke) proposes ADHD symptoms arise from insufficient DMN suppression during tasks.

**Seminal work:** "Toward Systems Neuroscience of ADHD: A Meta-Analysis of 55 fMRI Studies" (2012, *American Journal of Psychiatry*); "Characterizing Cognition in ADHD: Beyond Executive Dysfunction" (2006, *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*).

**Q1 — What is the network signature of ADHD?** Insufficient DMN suppression during cognitive engagement, reduced anticorrelation between DMN and task-positive networks, and increased temporal variability in DMN activity that intrudes on task processing. Stimulants partially normalize these dynamics.

**Q2 — Does chronic stimulant use produce lasting network changes?** Evidence is mixed. If stimulants facilitate more typical network dynamics during critical developmental periods, cascading benefits to network organization are possible. But DMN abnormalities persist even in treated adults, suggesting pharmacological normalization is largely state-dependent.

**Q3 — Can connectivity predict discontinuation success?** Baseline DMN-frontoparietal anticorrelation strength could serve as a biomarker. Individuals with stronger intrinsic network switching capacity may better tolerate discontinuation. His connectomic approach specifically aims to identify such brain-behavior signatures.

### 8. Tobias U. Hauser

**Affiliation:** Head, Developmental Computational Psychiatry Lab, University of Tübingen | **Discipline:** Developmental Computational Psychiatry

**Semantic frame — Neural gain as the unifying computational deficit:** Hauser proposes that ADHD's behavioral variability across domains emerges from impaired catecholamine-dependent neural gain — the amplification factor at synapses. Reduced gain equals more stochastic, variable behavior. Stimulants are gain-enhancing interventions that tighten the input-output function.

**Seminal work:** "Computational Psychiatry of ADHD: Neural Gain Impairments across Marrian Levels of Analysis" (2016, *Trends in Neurosciences*); "From symptom-based heterogeneity to mechanism-based profiling" (2025, *Neuropsychopharmacology*).

**Q1 — What is the core computational deficit?** Impaired neural gain manifesting as increased decision temperature, elevated behavioral variability, and reduced signal-to-noise ratio. Different ADHD subgroups may have primarily striatal DA impairment versus frontal NA impairment.

**Q2 — Can computational modeling predict who recovers after discontinuation?** Individuals whose ADHD is primarily gain-dependent would return to elevated variability. But developmental learning during treatment — enabled by improved gain — may persist as trained strategies. His computational phenotyping approach could identify who has reversible versus structural deficits.

**Q3 — Do different subgroups need different strategies?** Yes. Computational task batteries separately measuring striatal gain (reward learning) and frontal gain (attentional flexibility) could guide personalized treatment selection and discontinuation planning.

### 9. Edmund J.S. Sonuga-Barke

**Affiliation:** Professor of Developmental Psychology, Psychiatry and Neuroscience, King's College London | **Discipline:** Developmental Psychology / Dynamical Systems

**Semantic frame — Multi-pathway dynamical heterogeneity:** Sonuga-Barke conceptualizes ADHD as emerging from the dynamic interaction of semi-independent neurocognitive pathways — executive dysfunction (fronto-dorsal-striatal), delay aversion (fronto-ventral-striatal), and temporal processing deficits. His dynamical systems framing treats attention lapses as failures in brain switching dynamics rather than static deficits.

**Seminal work:** "Psychological heterogeneity in AD/HD: A dual pathway model" (2002, *Behavioural Brain Research*); Default Mode Interference Hypothesis (with Castellanos, 2007).

**Q1 — Is ADHD one disorder or many?** It is dynamically heterogeneous — different individuals are affected to different degrees across dissociable pathways. Stimulants likely have different effects on different pathways, meaning a single medication may not address all dimensions equally.

**Q2 — What does the DMI hypothesis predict about post-discontinuation?** The brain returns to its intrinsic pattern of disrupted DMN-task-positive fluctuations. However, if extended treatment modified the brain's attractor landscape — strengthened task-positive attractors — some switching capacity might persist. Environmental supports can compensate by reducing DMN intrusion.

**Q3 — Should we expect linear decline or nonlinear phase transitions after discontinuation?** His dynamical systems framing predicts potential nonlinearities — some patients may experience sudden shifts in functional organization rather than gradual decline, especially if medication had been stabilizing an inherently unstable dynamical system.

### 10. Anouk Scheres

**Affiliation:** Associate Professor, Behavioural Science Institute, Radboud University, The Netherlands | **Discipline:** Motivation Science / Self-Determination Theory

**Semantic frame — Internal motivation as the missing variable in ADHD research:** Scheres reframes executive dysfunction as fundamentally motivational, integrating Self-Determination Theory to argue that ADHD research has been overreliant on external reinforcement paradigms while ignoring autonomy, competence, and relatedness as mediators of self-regulation. Her MINE Project investigates how people with ADHD become disconnected from their "authentic inner compass."

**Seminal work:** "Studying Motivation in ADHD: The Role of Internal Motives and the Relevance of Self Determination Theory" (Morsink, Van der Oord, Antrop, Danckaerts, & Scheres, 2022, *J Attention Disorders*).

**Q1 — Do stimulants undermine intrinsic motivation?** Drawing on SDT's well-documented "undermining effect" of external rewards, stimulants may strengthen task performance while eroding autonomous self-regulation. Upon discontinuation, individuals face a double deficit: loss of pharmacological support AND an underdeveloped capacity for intrinsic motivation.

**Q2 — How should "recovery" be reconceptualized through a motivational lens?** Recovery should centrally include satisfaction of basic psychological needs — autonomy-supportive relationships, competence-building activities, relational connection — not merely cognitive task performance. Reconnecting with one's authentic motivational core is as important as restoring working memory.

**Q3 — Why do some adults function well without medication?** Context variability in ADHD is fundamentally motivational. Those who function well off medication have found environments and activities that satisfy basic psychological needs, enabling intrinsic motivation to scaffold executive function. Environmental fit may be more determinative than raw cognitive capacity.

### 11. Lidia Zylowska

**Affiliation:** Associate Professor of Psychiatry, University of Minnesota; Earl E. Bakken Center for Spirituality and Healing | **Discipline:** Contemplative Neuroscience / Integrative Psychiatry

**Semantic frame — Mindfulness as a complementary neural training system for ADHD self-regulation:** Zylowska developed the first mindfulness training program specifically for ADHD populations. Her core insight is that while stimulants enhance top-down pharmacological control, mindfulness builds bottom-up awareness and non-judgmental self-correction — a skill that persists after training ends.

**Seminal work:** "Mindfulness meditation training in adults and adolescents with ADHD: A feasibility study" (2008, *J Attention Disorders*) — first-ever study of mindfulness for ADHD; *Mindfulness for Adult ADHD: A Clinician's Guide* (2021, Guilford Press).

**Q1 — Can mindfulness bridge amphetamine discontinuation?** Yes. Mindfulness trains real-time awareness of attentional lapses and self-correction through a mechanism complementary to pharmacology. It should be initiated before discontinuation so the skill is established.

**Q2 — Where does mindfulness succeed where stimulants fail?** Emotion regulation. Stimulants primarily improve cognitive symptoms but leave emotional dysregulation — which Zylowska considers a core ADHD impairment — largely unaddressed. Mindfulness cultivates non-reactive awareness of emotional states and self-compassion.

**Q3 — What neural mechanisms overlap between mindfulness and stimulants?** Both converge on DMN regulation through different pathways — stimulants via dopaminergic enhancement, mindfulness via sustained attentional training that reduces DMN interference during task-focused states. The pathways are complementary, not redundant.

### 12. J.J. Sandra Kooij

**Affiliation:** Professor of Adult ADHD, Amsterdam UMC/VU University Medical Center; Head, Expertise Center Adult ADHD, PsyQ | **Discipline:** Sleep Science / Circadian Neuroscience

**Semantic frame — ADHD as a 24-hour circadian disorder:** Kooij has documented that **73–78%** of ADHD adults have delayed sleep phase syndrome with dim-light melatonin onset delayed approximately 90 minutes. She reconceptualizes ADHD as fundamentally intertwined with circadian disruption — and demonstrates that stimulants compound the problem by further delaying the clock.

**Seminal work:** "The role of the circadian system in the etiology and pathophysiology of ADHD: Time to redefine ADHD?" (Bijlenga, Vollebregt, Kooij & Arns, 2019, *ADHD Attention Deficit and Hyperactivity Disorders*); RCT showing chronotherapy reduces ADHD symptoms by 14% (2021, *Chronobiology International*).

**Q1 — Do amphetamines create hidden circadian debt?** Yes. Stimulants push the already-delayed circadian clock later, compounding chronic sleep deprivation that degrades executive function independently of ADHD neurobiology. Upon discontinuation, the circadian system doesn't immediately normalize — patients face severe circadian disorganization.

**Q2 — How much "executive dysfunction" is actually sleep deprivation?** A substantial and underestimated proportion. Treating the circadian component alone — through morning bright light, evening melatonin, fixed wake times — improved ADHD symptoms by 14% in her RCT. Sleep deprivation independently impairs every hallmark executive function.

**Q3 — Should chronotherapy be standard in ADHD treatment?** Emphatically yes, especially during discontinuation. Routine screening for circadian disturbances, chronotype assessment, implementation of fixed wake times, morning light exposure, and low-dose melatonin should be part of every treatment protocol.

### 13. Mary V. Solanto

**Affiliation:** Professor of Pediatrics and Psychiatry, Hofstra/Northwell Zucker School of Medicine | **Discipline:** Clinical Psychology / Executive Function Intervention

**Semantic frame — "Pills don't build skills" — metacognitive therapy as the missing intervention layer:** Solanto bridges stimulant pharmacology research (she co-edited *Stimulant Drugs and ADHD* with Arnsten and Castellanos) to psychosocial intervention, developing Meta-Cognitive Therapy that directly targets time management, organization, and planning — the executive skills medication cannot teach.

**Seminal work:** "Efficacy of Meta-Cognitive Therapy for Adult ADHD" (2010, *American Journal of Psychiatry*) — NIMH-funded RCT; *Cognitive-Behavioral Therapy for Adult ADHD: Targeting Executive Dysfunction* (2011, Guilford Press).

**Q1 — What happens when adults stop stimulants without compensatory skills?** Significant functional deterioration beyond baseline return. Adults who relied on stimulants for years may have never developed organizational strategies. They face both the return of cognitive symptoms AND the absence of compensatory skills — a double hit.

**Q2 — Can metacognitive training substitute for medication effects?** It produces genuine skill acquisition — clinically meaningful improvements in inattention, executive dysfunction, and organization that are statistically superior to support groups. These skills persist because they represent learned capacity, not pharmacological state.

**Q3 — How should treatment be sequenced?** Skills training should be foundational, not merely adjunctive to medication. Ideally, metacognitive skills should be built during periods of optimal pharmacological support so they are available as scaffolding when medication is reduced or discontinued.

### 14. Sanneke de Haan

**Affiliation:** Socrates Professor of Psychiatry and Philosophy, Erasmus University Rotterdam; Utrecht University | **Discipline:** Phenomenology / Enactivism / 4E Cognition

**Semantic frame — Psychiatric conditions as disorders of sense-making, not brain dysfunction:** De Haan applies enactivism to psychiatry, integrating experiential, neurophysiological, socio-cultural, and existential dimensions without reducing any to another. Her concept of "self-illness ambiguity" directly addresses the identity questions that arise when cognitive capacities depend on pharmaceutical intervention.

**Seminal work:** *Enactive Psychiatry* (2020, Cambridge University Press); "The phenomenology of deep brain stimulation-induced changes in OCD: an enactive affordance-based model" (with Rietveld, 2013, *Frontiers in Human Neuroscience*).

**Q1 — What does stimulant-enhanced executive function actually accomplish?** It shifts the person's entire sense-making system. Tasks that previously felt irrelevant begin to "solicit" engagement. Executive function recovery is not neural repair but re-attunement of the whole person-environment system across all four dimensions.

**Q2 — Is the medicated or unmedicated self "really you"?** The question is ill-formed. From an enactive perspective, both are authentically "you" — different modes of sense-making within different body-brain-world configurations. What matters is relational authenticity: whether the person can meaningfully engage with their values.

**Q3 — What does this imply for intervention design?** Interventions must target all dimensions — not just neurophysiology. Environment design changes the affordance landscape. Psychotherapy addresses existential dimensions. Socio-cultural interventions reshape context. Medication is valid but insufficient alone.

### 15. Neil Levy

**Affiliation:** Professor of Philosophy, Macquarie University; Senior Research Fellow, Uehiro Centre for Practical Ethics, Oxford | **Discipline:** Neuroethics / Philosophy of Mind

**Semantic frame — Extended cognition and the scaffolded model of autonomy:** Levy bridges the extended mind thesis with neuroethics, arguing that if cognition extends into the environment, then stimulant medication is a cognitive tool no different in kind from calendars, apps, or environmental scaffolds. His "scaffolded autonomy" model argues that autonomous decision-making always depends on distributed supports.

**Seminal work:** "Rethinking Neuroethics in the Light of the Extended Mind Thesis" (2007, *American Journal of Bioethics*); "Enhancing Authenticity" (2011, *J Applied Philosophy*); *Neuroethics* (2007, Cambridge University Press).

**Q1 — Does medication dependence undermine cognitive authenticity?** Emphatically no. Medication helps outward expression align with the person's deep capacities. The ADHD individual on medication is no less authentically themselves than a myopic person wearing glasses.

**Q2 — What is the conceptual status of stimulant medication as a cognitive tool?** Once we accept the extended mind thesis, the boundary between "internal" neural enhancement and "external" cognitive scaffolding becomes philosophically arbitrary. Restricting access to ADHD medication is restricting access to a cognitive scaffold needed for equitable functioning.

**Q3 — What does scaffolded autonomy imply for recovery programs?** The goal is not "pure internal self-regulation" but building a robust scaffolding ecology — medication, environmental supports, social accountability, technological aids. Recovery means scaffold optimization, not scaffold elimination.

### 16. Erik Rietveld

**Affiliation:** University of Amsterdam, Department of Philosophy; Co-founder, RAAAF | **Discipline:** Ecological Psychology / Affordance Theory

**Semantic frame — The Skilled Intentionality Framework and selective openness to affordances:** Rietveld's framework reframes executive dysfunction as dis-attunement between internal dynamics and the landscape of affordances. A person with ADHD doesn't lack executive function in the abstract — they fail to achieve "optimal grip" on the right affordances at the right time. The wrong affordances solicit them (distractions); goal-relevant affordances fail to generate action readiness.

**Seminal work:** "A Rich Landscape of Affordances" (Rietveld & Kiverstein, 2014, *Ecological Psychology*); "Self-organization, free energy minimization, and optimal grip on a field of affordances" (Bruineberg & Rietveld, 2014, *Frontiers in Human Neuroscience*).

**Q1 — What is executive dysfunction from this perspective?** A failure of selective openness — internal dynamics fail to discriminate between relevant and irrelevant affordances. Stimulants recalibrate selective openness, allowing the person to achieve optimal grip.

**Q2 — How should environments be designed for ADHD?** Reduce salience of distracting affordances; amplify solicitation power of goal-relevant affordances; design for existing skills rather than demanding capacities the person lacks. Create stable, structured environments with predictable affordance landscapes.

**Q3 — Why does medication work better for some tasks than others?** Novel tasks generate excessive prediction error (overwhelming); routine tasks generate too little (boring). Stimulants optimize precision-weighting of prediction errors, having maximal impact where attunement is most disrupted.

### 17. Anthony Chemero

**Affiliation:** Distinguished Research Professor of Philosophy and Psychology, University of Cincinnati | **Discipline:** Radical Embodied Cognitive Science / Dynamical Systems

**Semantic frame — Cognition as agent-environment coupling, executive function as a dynamical pattern:** Chemero provides the most thoroughgoing argument that cognition is entirely agent-environment dynamics describable using nonlinear dynamical systems theory. "Executive function" is not an internal module but a pattern of stable, flexible coordination between agent and environment. ADHD is an alternative mode of coupling that is maladaptive in certain environments but potentially adaptive in others.

**Seminal work:** *Radical Embodied Cognitive Science* (2009, MIT Press); "An Outline of a Theory of Affordances" (2003, *Ecological Psychology*).

**Q1 — What does "executive function" even refer to?** A dynamical property of the whole agent-environment system — the ability to maintain task-relevant coupling while flexibly adapting. Not a brain property. In ADHD, the system self-organizes differently, not "worse."

**Q2 — What does medication do from this perspective?** It changes the control parameters of the dynamical system, potentially shifting it from one attractor state to another — enabling a more stable pattern of task-relevant coordination. The medication doesn't create executive function; it changes parameters that allow functional coupling to emerge.

**Q3 — What about the concept of "cognitive deficit"?** The concept is environment-relative. A person with ADHD in a novelty-rich environment may outperform. The goal is not normalizing internal dynamics but optimizing agent-environment fit — sometimes through medication, sometimes through environmental redesign, ideally both.

### 18. Adina Maeir

**Affiliation:** Professor, School of Occupational Therapy, Hebrew University of Jerusalem | **Discipline:** Occupational Therapy / Cognitive Rehabilitation

**Semantic frame — Executive function as occupational performance and the Cog-Fun intervention architecture:** Maeir conceptualizes executive dysfunction as breakdown in the ability to carry out meaningful daily activities. Her Cognitive-Functional (Cog-Fun) intervention uses guided discovery and strategy practice within personally meaningful activities to build metacognitive awareness — creating an internal "operating system" to replace external supports.

**Seminal work:** Cog-Fun RCT (2017, *American Journal of Occupational Therapy*, N=107); Cog-Fun A adult pilot (2022, *AJOT*).

**Q1 — Can strategies transfer to daily life without medication?** Yes — transfer requires explicit metacognitive training embedded in ecologically valid, personally meaningful activities. The person must understand their own executive profile and practice strategies in real settings. Metacognition becomes the internal scaffolding replacing chemical scaffolding.

**Q2 — What role does environment play?** It is a primary therapeutic lever. Environmental modifications — reducing clutter, using timers, preparing materials — externalize executive demands, making the invisible visible and the effortful automatic.

**Q3 — Does medication status moderate treatment effects?** Her RCT found that Cog-Fun benefits were **not moderated by medication status**. This is a landmark finding: metacognitive-functional approaches build genuine compensatory capacity that operates independently of pharmacological enhancement.

### 19. Julia J. Rucklidge

**Affiliation:** Professor of Clinical Psychology, University of Canterbury, New Zealand; Director, Mental Health and Nutrition Research Group | **Discipline:** Nutritional Neuroscience

**Semantic frame — The undernourished brain and executive dysfunction as metabolic insufficiency:** Rucklidge's radical-in-simplicity frame: the brain requires adequate vitamins and minerals to manufacture neurotransmitters, maintain mitochondrial function, and manage oxidative stress. Stimulant-induced appetite suppression likely creates nutritional deficits that undermine the very cognitive functions medications are meant to enhance.

**Seminal work:** "Vitamin-Mineral Treatment of ADHD in Adults: Double-Blind Randomised Placebo-Controlled Trial" (2014, *British Journal of Psychiatry*, N=80); *The Better Brain* (2021, with Kaplan).

**Q1 — Can nutrition recover executive function compromised by stimulant-induced malnutrition?** Highly plausible and under-investigated. Broad-spectrum micronutrients doubled ADHD symptom improvement over placebo in her 8-week adult trial. Upon discontinuation, comprehensive micronutrient protocols should be first-line to replenish depleted metabolic cofactors for dopamine/NE synthesis.

**Q2 — Why does broad-spectrum work when single nutrients fail?** Nutritional synergy — nutrients don't work in isolation. B-vitamins are cofactors for neurotransmitter synthesis; zinc is essential for dopamine receptor function; magnesium modulates NMDA receptors. The brain's metabolic pathways are interconnected networks requiring multiple inputs simultaneously.

**Q3 — What about the gut-brain axis?** Children with ADHD have elevated bifidobacterium; micronutrient supplementation normalizes this while increasing microbial diversity. The gut-brain axis mediates neuroinflammation, neurotransmitter precursor availability, and vagal nerve signaling to executive control regions. Rebuilding gut health after stimulant use is an important recovery component.

### 20. John J. Ratey

**Affiliation:** Associate Clinical Professor of Psychiatry, Harvard Medical School; Founder, Sparking Life | **Discipline:** Exercise Neuroscience / Neuropsychiatry

**Semantic frame — Exercise as molecular medicine that operates through the same pathways as stimulants:** Ratey positions exercise as a pharmacological intervention that immediately elevates dopamine and norepinephrine while simultaneously triggering BDNF release, promoting neurogenesis, and strengthening prefrontal circuits. Complex movement additionally engages the cerebellum — a critical and underappreciated regulator of executive attention.

**Seminal work:** *Spark: The Revolutionary New Science of Exercise and the Brain* (2008); *ADHD 2.0* (with Hallowell, 2021).

**Q1 — Can exercise partially replace stimulants?** For a minority, exercise alone suffices. For most, exercise is a powerful adjunct that reduces required medication dose and addresses residual symptoms. Chronic exercise upregulates dopamine receptor density and BDNF production, creating lasting structural changes that stimulants do not.

**Q2 — What exercise is optimal?** Aerobic activity for 30+ minutes most days to elevate baseline neurotransmitter levels, combined with complex movement (martial arts, dance, team sports) that taxes the attention system by engaging the cerebellum. Moderate-to-vigorous intensity preferred, with frequent brief movement breaks throughout the day.

**Q3 — Is executive function fundamentally embodied?** Yes — the brain's attention system is a movement-coordination system repurposed for abstract thought. The cerebellum, basal ganglia, and PFC are fundamentally motor-regulatory structures. This explains why physical exercise improves cognitive executive function and why movement-based recovery may outperform purely cognitive training.

### 21. Betsy Hoza

**Affiliation:** Bishop Joyce Chair of Human Development, Professor of Psychological Science, University of Vermont | **Discipline:** Exercise Science / Developmental Psychopathology

**Semantic frame — Physical activity as developmental infrastructure for executive and social function:** Hoza brings empirical rigor to the exercise-ADHD intersection, demonstrating that aerobic fitness may buffer the associations between ADHD symptom severity and academic/social impairment. Her CATs on the Move program embeds executive function training within structured physical activity games — a dual-channel intervention.

**Seminal work:** "Using Physical Activity to Manage ADHD Symptoms: The State of the Evidence" (2016, *Current Psychiatry Reports*); aerobic fitness buffering study (2024, *J Psychoeducational Assessment*).

**Q1 — Is exercise a stand-alone ADHD treatment?** Not yet — the evidence supports it as adjunctive. But it consistently shows positive effects on attention, executive function, mood, and peer relations, and should be mandatory in any discontinuation protocol.

**Q2 — Why is cognitively engaging exercise superior?** Structured games require inhibitory control, working memory, and cognitive flexibility while being physically active — a dual-channel training effect where movement provides neurochemical benefits while cognitive demands exercise EF circuits directly.

**Q3 — Does fitness buffer ADHD impairment?** Her research suggests higher aerobic fitness protects against the worst functional consequences of ADHD. The mechanism is dual: fitness-related neurochemical changes improve baseline executive capacity, and participation in activity provides mastery experiences that build genuine competence.

### 22. Charlotte A.M. Cecil

**Affiliation:** Associate Professor, Erasmus Medical Centre, Rotterdam | **Discipline:** Epigenetics / Gene-Environment Interaction

**Semantic frame — DNA methylation as dynamic molecular chronology of ADHD development:** Cecil frames epigenetic markers not as static biomarkers but as temporally sensitive molecular systems capturing gene-environment interplay. Her key discovery: epigenetic associations with ADHD are strongest at birth and attenuate cross-sectionally in childhood — suggesting a critical prenatal epigenetic window.

**Seminal work:** "Epigenetics and ADHD: Reflections on Current Knowledge, Research Priorities and Translational Potential" (Cecil & Nigg, 2022, *Molecular Diagnosis & Therapy*); PACE consortium EWAS meta-analysis (2020, *Translational Psychiatry*).

**Q1 — Can birth methylation predict ADHD before symptoms emerge?** Neonatal DNA methylation shows stronger prospective associations than childhood measures. This is her "epigenetic timing effects" finding. Individual effect sizes are small, but multi-modal prediction models incorporating methylation data could eventually contribute to risk profiling.

**Q2 — Could stimulants themselves alter epigenetic markers?** This is a critical unknown. Medication use could confound cross-sectional epigenetic studies. DNAm patterns associated with ADHD may partly reflect consequences of medication rather than causes of the disorder. Medication-naïve samples and before-after treatment designs are urgently needed.

**Q3 — How far are we from personalized epigenetic medicine for ADHD?** Still in infancy. Methylation-based profiling will show greatest utility combined with genetic, molecular, environmental, and lifestyle data. Concrete clinical applications are years away, but the translational potential is real.

### 23. Ryan Smith

**Affiliation:** Research Associate Professor and PI, Laureate Institute for Brain Research, Tulsa, Oklahoma | **Discipline:** Predictive Processing / Active Inference / Computational Psychiatry

**Semantic frame — ADHD as aberrant precision-weighting in hierarchical predictive processing:** Smith reframes executive function as an emergent property of hierarchical prediction-error minimization. Attention is precision optimization — the brain assigning confidence weightings to predictions. ADHD involves underconfident prior beliefs that fail to suppress irrelevant sensory input. Stimulants are precision-optimizers, not mere attention boosters.

**Seminal work:** "Recent advances in the application of predictive coding and active inference models within clinical neuroscience" (2021, *Psychiatry and Clinical Neurosciences*); Trempler et al., "Ignore the glitch but mind the switch" (2023, *J Psychiatric Research*) — direct evidence that methylphenidate improves both flexibility and stability of prediction in ADHD.

**Q1 — What is executive function in the predictive processing framework?** Not a module but an emergent property. "Attention" is precision optimization. "Inhibition" is suppressing sensory-driven prediction errors in favor of top-down predictions. ADHD involves a systematic bias toward bottom-up processing — not a deficit per se but a different processing mode.

**Q2 — What happens to precision-weighting upon discontinuation?** Baseline precision imbalances re-emerge. Underconfident priors return, distractibility increases. The medication was tuning a computational parameter, not creating structural change. However, learning that occurred under optimized precision conditions may partially persist.

**Q3 — Can computational phenotyping guide personalized treatment?** Active inference models could identify individuals with primarily precision-weighting deficits (who benefit most from pharmacological dopamine optimization) versus those with generative-model deficits (who benefit more from cognitive-behavioral interventions restructuring internal models).

## Part IV: Synthesis

### Five cross-cutting themes that should inform recovery program design

**Theme 1: The "double scaffold" problem.** Multiple experts converge on a finding with immediate startup implications: adults who discontinue amphetamines after years of use face not merely a return to baseline executive function, but a double loss — the pharmacological scaffold AND the compensatory skills they never developed because medication masked the need. Solanto calls this the treatment gap; Scheres frames it as motivational underdevelopment; Barkley calls it the chronic performance deficit that medication only temporarily bridges. **Any recovery program must build the second scaffold — metacognitive skills, environmental supports, motivational alignment — before removing the first.**

**Theme 2: The circadian-metabolic axis is a neglected recovery lever.** Kooij's circadian research and Rucklidge's nutritional work reveal two biological systems that stimulants actively damage while treating ADHD symptoms. Amphetamines delay circadian rhythms in a population already phase-delayed by ~90 minutes, and suppress appetite in brains that may already be nutritionally compromised. Recovery programs that fail to address circadian re-alignment (morning light, evening melatonin, fixed wake times) and nutritional rehabilitation (broad-spectrum micronutrients, gut health) are missing the most immediately actionable biological interventions. These are also the interventions with the fastest evidence-to-implementation pipeline.

**Theme 3: Environment design is as valid an intervention as pharmacology.** The philosophical and ecological psychology experts — de Haan, Levy, Rietveld, Chemero — converge powerfully on this point, which the applied researchers (Maeir, Solanto) confirm empirically. From the Skilled Intentionality Framework, executive dysfunction is dis-attunement to the affordance landscape; fix the landscape and you change the functional outcome without touching the brain. Levy's extended mind thesis destigmatizes dependence on external supports. **A "personal operating system" for ADHD is not a workaround — it is cognition itself, externalized.** Recovery programs should dedicate as much design effort to structuring the user's physical, digital, and social environment as to any internal capacity-building.

**Theme 4: Exercise deserves protocol-level integration, not lifestyle-level suggestion.** The convergence of Ratey's neurochemical narrative, Hoza's empirical rigor, and the meta-meta-analytic effect size of **d = 0.93** makes the case incontrovertible: cognitively engaging physical exercise is the single most effective non-pharmacological intervention for executive function in ADHD. It operates through the same dopaminergic and noradrenergic pathways as stimulant medication, with the addition of BDNF-mediated neuroplasticity that drugs do not provide. Ratey's insight that executive function is fundamentally embodied — that the brain's attention system is a movement-coordination system repurposed for abstract thought — suggests that movement-based interventions may be more neurally aligned than purely cognitive training.

**Theme 5: The identity question is real and must be addressed therapeutically.** De Haan's "self-illness ambiguity," Scheres' "authentic inner compass," and Levy's "enhancing authenticity" argument all converge on a psychological experience that many ADHD adults report but that most clinical programs ignore: *Who am I without my medication?* This is not merely philosophical — it is a clinical barrier to successful discontinuation. Adults who have been medicated for years may have constructed their professional identity, relationships, and self-concept around their medicated cognitive capacities. Recovery programs need explicit identity-work components — phenomenological self-exploration, values clarification, narrative reconstruction — alongside the cognitive and biological interventions.

### Seven open questions that represent research and product opportunities

1. **No prospective adult discontinuation study exists.** The single largest evidence gap. Whoever generates longitudinal data on executive function trajectories after amphetamine discontinuation in adults will own the evidence base.

2. **Do learned computational strategies survive discontinuation?** Frank's models predict that corticostriatal gating habits may persist; Hauser's framework suggests computational phenotyping could predict who retains what. Neither has been tested clinically.

3. **What is the optimal sequencing of multimodal interventions during tapering?** Should metacognitive training, exercise protocols, chronotherapy, and nutritional supplementation begin weeks before tapering? Months? There is no evidence base for sequencing.

4. **Does the DAT upregulation Rubia warns about actually produce worse-than-baseline function?** PET studies of striatal DAT density before and after chronic stimulant use in ADHD adults would answer this critical question.

5. **Can mindfulness plus exercise produce neuroplastic changes equivalent to continued medication?** Both independently target DMN-executive network dynamics through distinct mechanisms. Their combination has never been tested against continued stimulant treatment.

6. **Do epigenetic marks predict discontinuation trajectories?** Cecil's framework suggests methylation profiles could identify individuals with more versus less biologically entrenched ADHD phenotypes.

7. **How should digital tools scaffold the transition?** Levy's extended mind thesis suggests that well-designed digital tools are literally part of the user's cognitive system. The product design question is: what digital affordances best replace the precision-weighting function that stimulants provide?

### What this means for building an ADHD management startup

The topology of expertise mapped in this report points toward a recovery program architecture with five integrated layers, each informed by specific expert frames:

- **Layer 1 — Biological foundation:** Circadian re-alignment (Kooij), nutritional rehabilitation (Rucklidge), exercise protocols (Ratey, Hoza). These are the fastest to implement and address the biological systems stimulants actively damage. Begin before medication tapering.

- **Layer 2 — Metacognitive skill building:** Structured executive function training (Solanto, Maeir) delivered during periods of continued or partial pharmacological support, so skills are built while cognitive resources are available. Brown's six-cluster model provides the assessment framework; Barkley's point-of-performance concept guides when and where to deploy skills.

- **Layer 3 — Environment and scaffold design:** Affordance landscape engineering (Rietveld), extended cognitive system design (Levy), occupational environment modification (Maeir). Physical spaces, digital tools, and social structures designed to externalize executive demands and amplify goal-relevant affordances.

- **Layer 4 — Motivational and identity work:** SDT-informed autonomy support (Scheres), contemplative self-awareness (Zylowska), phenomenological identity exploration (de Haan). Reconnecting with intrinsic motivation and reconstructing a coherent sense of self that does not depend on pharmacological cognitive state.

- **Layer 5 — Ongoing monitoring and personalization:** Computational phenotyping (Frank, Hauser, Smith) to identify individual profiles of gain impairment, gating capacity, and precision-weighting. Network connectivity biomarkers (Castellanos) to predict discontinuation trajectories. Surman's treatment gap metrics to assess whether functional EF, not just symptom scores, is being maintained.

The experts profiled here do not merely represent different opinions. They represent different **levels of the same system** — from molecular synapses (Arnsten) through computational algorithms (Frank) through brain networks (Castellanos) through agent-environment dynamics (Rietveld) through lived experience (de Haan) through social context (Scheres). A recovery program that operates at all these levels simultaneously, sequenced intelligently and personalized computationally, would represent a genuine frontier advance in ADHD care — one that no single expert's frame can achieve alone, but that their collective intelligence makes possible.
