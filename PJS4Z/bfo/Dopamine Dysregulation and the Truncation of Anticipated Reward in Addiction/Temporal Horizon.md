# Temporal Horizon

**Definition:** The cognitive boundary or temporal window within which an organism can spontaneously project conscious thought, simulate, and value outcomes that have not yet occurred.

## Systems Dynamics & Relationships
The [[Temporal Horizon]] serves as the foundational cognitive canvas required for long-term decision making. In healthy neurologies, it extends years into the future, but in states of severe addiction, it undergoes severe truncation (averaging just 9 days for opioid-dependent individuals). This boundary is constricted biologically by [[D2 Receptor Downregulation]] and can be artificially stretched via [[Neural Coupling]] in the brain's executive centers.
- **Upstream (Driven by):** [[D2 Receptor Downregulation]] (decreases), [[Neural Coupling]] (increases)
- **Downstream (Drives):** [[Delay Discounting]] (decreases)

## Behavioral Engineering Context (COM-B)
In the COM-B model, a normative [[Temporal Horizon]] is a critical **Capability** driver. Without it, an individual lacks the psychological capacity to value delayed health or social outcomes, making the choice of abstinence computationally irrational. Leverage points to address a truncated horizon involve either expanding psychological capability via [[Episodic Future Thinking (EFT)]] or bypassing the deficit by restructuring **Opportunity**—delivering immediate rewards within the individual's shortened functional window via [[Contingency Management (CM)]].
