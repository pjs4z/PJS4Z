To investigate the portrayal of alcoholism and addiction in mainstream Western film, television, and literature, we must translate qualitative narrative tropes into a quantitative conceptual framework. Over the last four decades, Western media has largely transitioned from the **Moral Model** of addiction (viewing it as a failure of character or inherent badness) to the **Clinical/Trauma Model** (viewing it as a biopsychosocial disease). However, because storytelling requires conflict, the behavioral symptoms of addiction—impulsivity, deceit, and systemic instability—remain the engines of the plot.

To fulfill your objective, we can map the recurring persona archetypes—both the characters who embody the addiction and the "orbiters" affected by it—onto a continuous three-dimensional semantic space ranging from **-1.0 to +1.0**:

1. **Executive Function (EF):** `-1.0` (Total cognitive hijack, impulsivity, loss of agency) $\leftrightarrow$ `+1.0` (Hyper-competence, rigorous planning, masterful control).
    
2. **Morality (MOR):** `-1.0` (Fundamentally selfish, manipulative, destructive) $\leftrightarrow$ `+1.0` (Altruistic, virtuous, self-sacrificing).
    
3. **Emotional Valence (EV):** `-1.0` (Profound suffering, shame, despair, agony) $\leftrightarrow$ `+1.0` (Euphoria, joy, transcendent peace).
    

By defining the predominant archetypes and assigning them estimated cultural prevalence weights based on modern Western storytelling, we can calculate a **weighted average semantic representation** of the addiction ecosystem.

### Part I: Archetypes of the Addicted Persona

Mainstream media generally relies on four core archetypes to embody the addict.

**1. The Tragic Victim / Spiraling Sufferer (Weight: 40%)**

- **Examples:** Rue Bennett (_Euphoria_), Nic Sheff (_Beautiful Boy_), Ben Sanderson (_Leaving Las Vegas_), Bubbles (_The Wire_).
    
- **Profile:** The modern prestige default driven by the disease model. The character's brain is biologically hijacked. They desperately want to get clean but fail repeatedly. Their actions cause immense harm, but the narrative frames this as a symptom of the illness rather than inherent malice.
    
- **Semantic Vector:** `EF: -0.8` | `MOR: 0.0` | `EV: -0.8`
    

**2. The High-Functioning Antihero / Tortured Genius (Weight: 30%)**

- **Examples:** Don Draper (_Mad Men_), Beth Harmon (_The Queen's Gambit_), BoJack Horseman, Dr. Gregory House (_House M.D._).
    
- **Profile:** A uniquely Western romanticization. The substance is used to quiet a hyper-active mind or dull the existential pain of brilliance. They maintain incredibly high executive function in their professional domain (compartmentalization), but their personal lives rot.
    
- **Semantic Vector:** `EF: +0.3` | `MOR: -0.3` | `EV: -0.5`
    

**3. The Destructive Chaos Agent (Weight: 20%)**

- **Examples:** Frank Gallagher (_Shameless_), Howard Ratner (_Uncut Gems_), Mark Renton (_Trainspotting_ - early acts).
    
- **Profile:** In this archetype, the addiction entirely strips away empathy. They lie, steal from their families, and manipulate. Their executive function is purely opportunistic—cunning at acquiring drugs or money, but incapable of long-term life management.
    
- **Semantic Vector:** `EF: -0.6` | `MOR: -0.8` | `EV: -0.3` _(Features brief manic highs, but mostly lows)_
    

**4. The Comedic Degenerate (Weight: 10%)**

- **Examples:** The Dude (_The Big Lebowski_), Arthur Bach (_Arthur_), Homer Simpson.
    
- **Profile:** A historically significant but fading trope where intoxication is played for laughs. The character is a bumbling fool whose drunkenness represents a harmless detachment from societal norms.
    
- **Semantic Vector:** `EF: -0.5` | `MOR: +0.3` | `EV: +0.4`

> - **EF:** `(-0.8 × 0.40) + (0.3 × 0.30) + (-0.6 × 0.20) + (-0.5 × 0.10)` = **-0.40**

> - **MOR:** `(0.0 × 0.40) + (-0.3 × 0.30) + (-0.8 × 0.20) + (0.3 × 0.10)` = **-0.22**
>     
> - **EV:** `(-0.8 × 0.40) + (-0.5 × 0.30) + (-0.3 × 0.20) + (0.4 × 0.10)` = **-0.49**

> 
> **Addict Final Representation:** `[EF: -0.40, MOR: -0.22, EV: -0.49]`

### Part II: Archetypes of the Affected (The Orbiters)

Addiction in narrative acts as a black hole, violently warping the orbits of surrounding characters. They typically fall into three archetypes:

**1. The Exhausted Savior / Martyr (Weight: 45%)**

- **Examples:** Skyler White (_Breaking Bad_ - early seasons), Ally Maine (_A Star is Born_), David Sheff (_Beautiful Boy_).
    
- **Profile:** The spouse, parent, or sponsor who dedicates their life to preventing the addict's world from collapsing. They are forced to become highly organized to compensate for the addict's chaos. They are morally elevated but drowning in anticipatory grief.
    
- **Semantic Vector:** `EF: +0.8` | `MOR: +0.7` | `EV: -0.6`
    

**2. The Parentified / Traumatized Dependent (Weight: 35%)**

- **Examples:** Fiona Gallagher (_Shameless_), Jeannette Walls (_The Glass Castle_).
    
- **Profile:** The innocent bystander (often a child) forced to mature prematurely to survive the chaos generated by the addict. They exhibit compensatory executive function out of sheer survival instinct and absolute moral innocence, but carry profound, quiet trauma.
    
- **Semantic Vector:** `EF: +0.6` | `MOR: +0.8` | `EV: -0.7`
    

**3. The Co-dependent Enabler (Weight: 20%)**

- **Examples:** Carmela Soprano (_The Sopranos_), Christopher Moltisanti’s mafia circle (_The Sopranos_), Layla (_Buffalo '66_).
    
- **Profile:** They love the addict but subconsciously facilitate the addiction to maintain the status quo, out of fear of confrontation, or to justify their own vices. They are morally gray—complicit but victimized.
    
- **Semantic Vector:** `EF: -0.2` | `MOR: -0.2` | `EV: -0.4`
    

> **The Affected Vector (Weighted Average):**
> 
> - **EF:** `(0.8 × 0.45) + (0.6 × 0.35) + (-0.2 × 0.20)` = **+0.53**
>     
> - **MOR:** `(0.7 × 0.45) + (0.8 × 0.35) + (-0.2 × 0.20)` = **+0.56**
>     
> - **EV:** `(-0.6 × 0.45) + (-0.7 × 0.35) + (-0.4 × 0.20)` = **-0.60**
>     
> 
> **Affected Final Representation:** `[EF: +0.53, MOR: +0.56, EV: -0.60]`

### Part III: Cultural Synthesis and Interpretation

By placing these two quantitative vectors side-by-side—**The Addict** `[-0.40, -0.22, -0.49]` and **The Affected** `[+0.53, +0.56, -0.60]`—the underlying architectural formula of the Western addiction narrative is revealed:

**1. The Parasitic Transfer of Executive Function (`-0.40` vs `+0.53`)**

The semantic data reveals an almost perfect teeter-totter of human agency. Addiction is fundamentally portrayed in media as the _involuntary outsourcing of executive function_. As the addict's ability to regulate their life drops into a moderate deficit (`-0.40`), the narrative forces the people around them into a state of compensatory, unsustainable hyper-competence (`+0.53`) just to keep the family, workplace, or relationship from systemic collapse.

**2. The Neutralization of Moral Villainy (`-0.22` vs `+0.56`)**

Historically, the alcoholic was the villain (`MOR: -1.0`). Today, the addict's weighted morality hovers at a mildly compromised `-0.22`. This perfectly captures modern media's tightrope walk: the addict is no longer viewed as purely "evil," but rather as a morally neutralized agent whose hijacked brain forces them into bad actions. Because the addict is morally ambiguous, the Affected are granted a halo of tragic virtue (`+0.56`), serving as the audience's undeniable moral anchors.

**3. The Asymmetrical Emotional Black Hole (`-0.49` vs `-0.60`)**

The most striking finding in this semantic approximation is the emotional valence. Mainstream Western media almost universally rejects the idea that addiction is "fun." Aside from brief montages of fleeting highs, addiction is semantically coded as a localized natural disaster.

Despite the addict being the one who actively chases the chemical reward system, their aggregate emotional baseline (`-0.49`) is functionally identical to—and even slightly eclipsed by—the despair of the collateral victims around them (`-0.60`). The ultimate cultural representation of addiction is one where the addict occasionally experiences the pharmacological relief or oblivion of the substance, but the Affected are entirely sober, present, and unmedicated for the duration of the nightmare.
